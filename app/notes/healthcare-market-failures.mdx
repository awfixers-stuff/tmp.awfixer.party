## Healthcare Market Failures

The American healthcare system is a case study in market failure — and the failures aren't accidental. They're the predictable result of incentive structures that reward consolidation, not competition.

---

## The Promise vs. Reality

**The Promise:** Free-market healthcare with competition driving down prices and improving quality

**The Reality:** Hospital mergers raise prices 20-40%. Insurance consolidation leaves many regions with one or two carriers. The "competitive" healthcare market produces some of the highest costs in the world.

This isn't a failure of markets. It's a failure to understand how markets actually work in this industry.

---

## Why Healthcare Doesn't Behave Like Other Markets

### Information Asymmetry

In a normal market, consumers can compare prices and quality. In healthcare:

- You don't choose when you need care
- You can't easily compare prices
- Your doctor makes the consumption decision, not you

This isn't like buying a car. It's like being told you need a new engine and having to trust the mechanic's diagnosis.

### Consolidation Economics

When hospitals merge, they don't compete harder. They raise prices. Studies consistently show:

- Hospital merger markets see 20-40% price increases
- The merged entity gains pricing power
- Competitors exit or are acquired

This is the opposite of how competition is supposed to work. But it's entirely predictable when you understand the incentive: in healthcare, scale is more profitable than competition.

### Regulatory Capture

The agencies supposed to protect competition are often staffed by people from the industries they regulate. The "antitrust" framework that nominally protects competition has repeatedly failed to block consolidation.

---

## The Pharmaceutical Problem

The pharmaceutical industry operates in a deliberately insulated market:

- The government is *legally prohibited* from negotiating Medicare drug prices in bulk
- This carve-out was written by legislators who receive pharmaceutical donations
- The result: Americans pay 2-3x what people in other countries pay for the same drugs

This isn't a market failure. It's a *political* failure producing a market that serves the politically powerful rather than the public.

---

## What AWFixer Proposes

1. **Aggressive Antitrust Enforcement** — Actually block the mergers that raise prices

2. **Medicare Price Negotiation** — Allow the government to negotiate drug prices

3. **Address Regulatory Capture** — Cooling-off periods, conflict-of-interest disclosure, independent oversight

4. **Transparent Pricing** — Require price disclosure so consumers can actually comparison shop

The healthcare market isn't working because the incentive structure doesn't reward competition. Change the incentives, change the outcomes.
