# AWFixer Party — Technology & AI Policy

---

## PILLAR TEN: Technology and Artificial Intelligence — Open Systems, Accountable Algorithms, and the Right to Curiosity

---

### The AWFixer Core Argument

The most consequential technological transformation in human history is currently underway, and the United States government's response has been a combination of bewildered congressional hearings, toothless voluntary commitments extracted from companies with no intention of honoring them, and the quiet embedding of opaque automated systems into public institutions that affect millions of lives without any meaningful accountability to the people those systems affect.

Meanwhile, the legal framework governing the relationship between citizens and technology was written in a different era, for different problems, and has been systematically interpreted by courts and prosecutors in ways that criminalize curiosity, protect corporate opacity, and treat the act of understanding how a system works as equivalent to attacking it.

AWFixer's technology policy is built on two foundational convictions that most politicians are unwilling to state plainly:

**First:** Any system that exercises power over people must be understandable by those people or their representatives. This is not a technology principle. It is a democracy principle that technology has made newly urgent.

**Second:** Curiosity is not a crime. The impulse to understand how things work — to probe, to test, to find the edges of a system — is one of the most valuable human traits civilization possesses. A legal framework that treats this impulse as criminal depending on whose system is being examined does not protect security. It protects incumbency.

These two convictions lead directly to the positions that follow.

### The Honest Assessment of Where We Are

**AI in Government: Power Without Accountability**

Artificial intelligence systems are currently used across American government to make or substantially influence decisions about:

- Who receives bail and at what amount
- Who is flagged for welfare fraud investigation
- Which neighborhoods receive increased police patrols
- Who is identified as a potential terrorist or security threat
- Which veterans receive disability benefits and at what level
- Which students are flagged as at-risk or disciplinary concerns
- Which job applicants receive interviews at agencies using automated screening

In essentially every one of these cases, the algorithm making or influencing the decision is:
- Proprietary — owned by a private vendor and protected as a trade secret
- Opaque — neither the affected individual nor their legal representative can examine how the decision was reached
- Unaudited — no independent body has verified that the system performs as claimed or that its outputs are free from discriminatory bias
- Unaccountable — when the system produces a wrong outcome, there is no clear mechanism for remedy and no clear party bearing responsibility

This is not a theoretical concern. Risk assessment algorithms used in criminal sentencing have been shown to systematically overpredict recidivism risk for Black defendants and underpredict it for white defendants — producing racially disparate sentencing recommendations that judges have treated as objective while the methodology producing them was shielded from scrutiny as a trade secret. Predictive policing systems have embedded existing enforcement disparities into algorithmic outputs and presented them as neutral predictions. Automated benefits determination systems have cut off healthcare and disability payments to vulnerable people based on errors that took years to identify and correct because the system's logic was inaccessible.

A government making decisions about its citizens' liberty, benefits, and safety using tools it cannot explain and that no one is permitted to examine has abandoned the most basic requirements of due process. AWFixer intends to end this.

**AI in the Private Sector: Concentrated Power, Diffuse Risk**

The private sector AI landscape is characterized by an extreme concentration of capability in a small number of companies — primarily those with access to the computational infrastructure, training data, and talent that modern large-scale AI development requires. This concentration creates several distinct problems:

The companies with the most powerful AI systems have every incentive to keep those systems opaque. Opacity protects competitive advantage. It also protects against accountability for harms — a system whose operation cannot be examined cannot be proven to have caused a specific harm in a specific case.

The downstream effects of private AI systems extend far beyond the companies deploying them. Credit scoring algorithms affect millions of people's access to housing and capital. Social media recommendation algorithms have been extensively documented to amplify rage, misinformation, and in some cases content that facilitates real-world harm. Hiring algorithms screen hundreds of millions of job applications. Insurance pricing algorithms determine who can afford coverage and at what cost. None of these systems are publicly accountable. All of them exercise power over people's lives.

The legal framework — primarily the Computer Fraud and Abuse Act of 1986 and a patchwork of state equivalents — has been used extensively to prevent the independent security and accountability research that would allow these harms to be identified and documented. Researchers who probe these systems for vulnerabilities or bias face criminal prosecution under statutes written before the commercial internet existed, applied to conduct their authors never contemplated.

### PRINCIPLE ONE: Public Sector AI Must Be Open Source — No Exceptions

This is the clearest and most non-negotiable position in the AWFixer technology platform.

**The Principle:**

Any artificial intelligence system, algorithm, automated decision tool, or machine learning model used by any agency, department, court, law enforcement body, or other governmental entity at the federal, state, or local level — or used by any private contractor performing a governmental function — must be:

- **Open source:** The complete source code, training methodology, and model architecture must be publicly available under an open license that permits examination, audit, and independent replication
- **Documented:** Full technical documentation including training data sources, known limitations, performance benchmarks, and bias audit results must be published and maintained
- **Audited:** Subject to independent technical audit before deployment and on a regular schedule thereafter, with audit results published publicly
- **Explainable:** Capable of producing a human-readable explanation for any individual decision — the affected person or their representative must be able to understand why the system reached the conclusion it did
- **Contestable:** Every decision made by or substantially influenced by an automated system must carry a right of human review by an official with authority to override the system's recommendation

**Why This Is Non-Negotiable:**

The argument against open-sourcing government AI is almost always framed as a security concern — if the source code is public, bad actors can find vulnerabilities and exploit them. This argument has merit in narrow specific contexts, such as systems directly involved in classified operations or active security infrastructure.

It does not have merit as a general argument against transparency in systems that affect citizens' rights and benefits. The security of a bail algorithm does not depend on keeping its methodology secret. The security of a benefits determination system is not compromised by public knowledge of how it calculates eligibility. The claim that government AI must be opaque for security reasons is, in the vast majority of applications, a vendor's intellectual property argument wearing a security costume.

AWFixer's response to genuine security concerns is the same as the response to national security classification in the fiscal transparency pillar: a narrow, independently reviewed carve-out for systems where operational security genuinely requires it, with that carve-out subject to oversight by the IACC and automatic review on a defined timeline. The default is transparency. Security exemptions are exceptions that require justification.

**Vendor Contracts:**

Government contracts for AI services are restructured so that:
- Any model or algorithm developed specifically for government use is owned by the government and open-sourced as a public asset
- Vendors who wish to sell AI services to the government must agree to open-source disclosure of any model deployed in government contexts
- Existing contracts are renegotiated on a defined timeline — vendors who refuse open-source terms lose government business

This will cost money in the short term, as some vendors will walk away or demand premium pricing for transparency compliance. AWFixer accepts this cost as the price of accountable government. It will also create a growing body of publicly available, government-funded AI development that benefits the broader research community and reduces long-term dependency on proprietary vendors.

### PRINCIPLE TWO: Private Sector AI — Burdens That Scale With Impact

AWFixer believes in the free market, in private innovation, and in the right of companies to protect genuine trade secrets. We also believe that when private systems exercise power over people's lives at scale, the public interest in understanding those systems overrides the private interest in protecting them.

The framework AWFixer proposes for private sector AI is not a blanket open-source mandate. It is a **tiered accountability system where the burden of transparency scales with the scale and nature of the impact.**

**Tier One: High-Impact Systems — Maximum Transparency Requirements**

Systems that meet any of the following criteria are classified as High-Impact and subject to the most stringent requirements:

- Used to make or substantially influence decisions affecting individual rights, benefits, employment, housing, credit, insurance, or liberty
- Deployed at a scale affecting more than 100,000 people annually
- Used in healthcare diagnosis, treatment recommendation, or insurance coverage determination
- Used in content moderation affecting public discourse at significant scale
- Used in financial markets in ways that affect systemic stability

**Requirements for Tier One systems:**

- **Algorithmic Impact Assessment** — mandatory public disclosure before deployment, updated annually, covering the system's purpose, methodology, known limitations, bias testing results, and potential for harm
- **Source code escrow** — the full source code and model weights are held by an independent technical escrow body accessible to qualified researchers, regulators, and in litigation where the system's outputs are at issue
- **Independent bias audit** — conducted by accredited third parties with no financial relationship to the deploying company, published publicly, repeated annually
- **Right of explanation** — any individual affected by a Tier One system's decision has a legal right to a meaningful explanation of how that decision was reached, in plain language
- **Liability attachment** — companies deploying Tier One systems bear strict liability for documented harms caused by those systems, without the current requirement that plaintiffs demonstrate the system's internal workings — which they cannot do if those workings are secret

**Financial Burden for Non-Compliance:**

AWFixer does not believe in regulatory requirements that companies simply price in as a cost of doing business. The financial penalties for Tier One non-compliance are:

- Fines calculated as a **percentage of global revenue** — not a fixed dollar amount that is trivial for large companies — starting at 4% of global annual revenue for first violations, scaling to 10% for repeat violations
- **Private right of action** — individuals harmed by non-compliant systems can sue directly, without requiring government enforcement action first
- **Injunctive relief** — courts can order a non-compliant system taken offline until compliance is achieved
- **Personal liability** for executives who knowingly certify compliance with false information — the same Sarbanes-Oxley principle applied to AI governance

**Tier Two: Significant Systems — Moderate Transparency Requirements**

Systems that affect fewer people or in less consequential domains face lighter requirements: algorithmic impact assessment, bias testing, and source code escrow without public disclosure. The goal is proportionality — not drowning small AI deployments in compliance overhead while ensuring that genuinely consequential systems are accountable.

**Tier Three: General Commercial Systems — Baseline Requirements**

All AI systems deployed commercially face a baseline: honest disclosure of the fact that AI is being used, prohibition on using AI-generated content to deceive people about the nature of who they are interacting with, and participation in a voluntary transparency registry that creates reputational incentives for proactive disclosure.

**The Open Source Incentive Structure:**

Rather than only punishing opacity, AWFixer creates positive incentives for open-source AI development:

- **Tax credits** for companies that open-source AI models, training data, and methodologies under qualifying open licenses
- **Federal procurement preference** — when capabilities are comparable, the government buys from companies with open-source AI practices
- **Research funding** directed primarily toward open-source AI development through universities, national laboratories, and independent research institutions
- **Antitrust consideration** — open-source AI contributions are considered as a factor in merger review, recognizing that closed AI ecosystems controlled by dominant players represent a form of market concentration with public consequences

### PRINCIPLE THREE: Legalized Hacking — Curiosity Is Not a Crime

This is the position that will generate the most controversy and that AWFixer is most committed to defending.

**The Current Situation:**

The Computer Fraud and Abuse Act of 1986 — written when the commercial internet did not exist, when a personal computer had less processing power than a modern thermostat, and when the concept of cloud computing was science fiction — is the primary federal law governing unauthorized access to computer systems. It defines "unauthorized access" so broadly that it has been used to prosecute:

- Security researchers who discovered and reported critical vulnerabilities in systems affecting millions of users
- Journalists who accessed public-facing websites in ways the website owner didn't prefer
- A young man who wrote a program to download academic articles too quickly from a database he had legitimate access to — Aaron Swartz, who faced 35 years in federal prison for this and took his own life before trial
- Researchers who studied discrimination in online advertising by creating test accounts
- People who violated a website's terms of service

The CFAA's fundamental problem is its treatment of **authorization** as a binary determined entirely by the system owner. If the owner says you're not authorized, any access — regardless of how it was achieved, what was done with it, or what harm if any resulted — is potentially criminal. This framework protects incumbents from scrutiny far more effectively than it protects the public from harm.

It has also, perversely, made America less secure. The security research community — the people who find the vulnerabilities that nation-state hackers and criminal organizations are also looking for — operates under legal threat when doing the work that makes systems safer. Researchers who discover a critical vulnerability in widely-used infrastructure face a choice between reporting it through channels that may result in prosecution and staying silent. Many stay silent. The vulnerability remains. The public is less safe.

This is not a hypothetical. Multiple critical security vulnerabilities in widely-used systems have been discovered first by malicious actors rather than defensive researchers because the legal environment made defensive research too risky. The public paid the cost of this in data breaches, ransomware attacks, and compromised infrastructure.

**The AWFixer Hacking Framework:**

AWFixer proposes to replace the CFAA with a modern computer security framework built on three principles: **intent, harm, and disclosure.**

*Intent matters.* The current framework treats the system owner's authorization as the only relevant fact. The AWFixer framework treats the researcher's intent as equally relevant. A person who accesses a system to find and report vulnerabilities is not the same as a person who accesses it to steal data or cause damage. The law should reflect this distinction.

*Harm matters.* Access to a system that produces no harm — no data stolen, no service disrupted, no privacy violated — is categorically different from access that produces concrete damage. The AWFixer framework calibrates consequences to actual harm rather than to the theoretical possibility of harm that the system owner's authorization framework implies.

*Disclosure is the goal.* The purpose of a computer security legal framework should be to produce a more secure technological environment. That means incentivizing the discovery and responsible disclosure of vulnerabilities, not criminalizing it.

**Specific AWFixer Positions:**

- **Repeal and replace the CFAA** with a modern statute that defines criminal computer access by reference to intent and harm rather than solely by reference to system owner authorization
- **Establish a Good Faith Security Research Safe Harbor** — researchers who access systems in good faith, for the purpose of identifying security vulnerabilities, without causing harm or accessing private data beyond what is necessary to demonstrate the vulnerability, and who disclose findings responsibly, are explicitly exempt from criminal prosecution and civil liability under the new framework
- **Responsible Disclosure Framework** — the law establishes a standard 90-day disclosure timeline: a researcher who discovers a vulnerability notifies the system owner privately, the owner has 90 days to patch, after which the researcher may publish their findings. System owners who retaliate against researchers during the disclosure period face legal consequences
- **Bug Bounty Infrastructure** — the federal government establishes and funds a national bug bounty program covering all federal systems, with compensation scaled to the severity of discovered vulnerabilities. Finding a critical vulnerability in federal infrastructure is rewarded, not prosecuted
- **Extend the safe harbor to journalism** — researchers are not the only people who need to probe systems to serve the public interest. Journalists investigating algorithmic discrimination, privacy violations, or platform manipulation deserve the same legal protection as security researchers when their methods involve technical investigation of publicly accessible systems

**The Reward Structure:**

Curiosity should not merely be decriminalized — it should be actively rewarded. AWFixer proposes:

- **Federal Bug Bounty Program** with substantial payouts for critical vulnerabilities in federal systems — the cost of paying researchers is trivially small compared to the cost of the breaches that undiscovered vulnerabilities produce
- **Critical Infrastructure Bounty Program** — extended to privately owned critical infrastructure including power grids, water systems, financial systems, and healthcare networks, funded through a combination of federal funds and mandatory industry contribution
- **Academic research grants** for security research, AI safety research, and algorithmic accountability research conducted openly and published publicly
- **Public recognition** — a formal mechanism for acknowledging security researchers who make significant contributions to public security, equivalent to the recognition given to other forms of public service

**The Limits:**

AWFixer is not proposing that hacking be consequence-free. The framework retains serious criminal penalties for:

- Access with intent to steal, damage, extort, or harm
- Access to systems containing genuinely sensitive personal data beyond what is necessary to demonstrate a vulnerability
- Disclosure of vulnerabilities to malicious third parties rather than to system owners or responsible public channels
- Nation-state or organized criminal hacking regardless of stated intent
- Any access to systems controlling physical infrastructure — power grids, water systems, medical devices — where the risk of unintended harm is too high to permit even good-faith research without explicit coordination with system operators

The distinction AWFixer is drawing is between **curiosity and malice, between discovery and exploitation, between the researcher and the criminal.** These are not difficult distinctions to draw. They are distinctions the current law deliberately refuses to make because the current law was written by and for the protection of system owners, not the public.

### PRINCIPLE FOUR: Data Ownership and Platform Accountability

**Data Ownership:**

The current framework treats personal data as property of the platform that collects it. AWFixer proposes inverting this:

- **Personal data belongs to the individual it describes.** Companies may collect and use personal data under license from the individual, subject to meaningful consent — not a 47-page terms of service agreement that no one reads, but a clear, plain-language disclosure of what data is collected, how it is used, and who it is shared with
- **Right of deletion** is absolute — any individual can demand the deletion of their personal data from any commercial system, with enforcement through the IACC and private right of action
- **Data portability** — individuals can export their complete data profile from any platform in a machine-readable format, enabling genuine competition between platforms rather than the lock-in that collected behavioral data currently creates
- **Data broker regulation** — the industry that exists to aggregate and sell personal data without direct relationships with the individuals profiled is brought under the same framework, with the same consent, deletion, and portability requirements

**Platform Accountability:**

- **Algorithmic accountability for recommendation systems** — platforms that use algorithmic recommendation to drive content consumption are subject to Tier One requirements: independent audit, bias testing, and source code escrow
- **End of dark pattern immunity** — interface designs deliberately constructed to manipulate users into choices they would not otherwise make — consent dark patterns, engagement manipulation, addictive design — are treated as deceptive practices under consumer protection law
- **Children's algorithmic protection** — platforms that knowingly recommend content to users under 16 face strict liability for documented harms from those recommendations, with no safe harbor for algorithmic amplification of harmful content to minors

### PRINCIPLE FIVE: AI and the Labor Market

AWFixer's technology policy does not exist in isolation from the labor policy described in Pillar Five. The displacement of workers by AI and automation is real, accelerating, and inadequately addressed by both the technology industry and the government.

AWFixer's position is neither to resist automation — which is futile — nor to pretend it is consequence-free — which is dishonest. It is to ensure that the productivity gains from automation are broadly shared rather than entirely captured by capital:

- **Automation impact disclosure** — companies above a defined size that deploy AI systems replacing human workers are required to disclose the scope of displacement and their retraining and transition commitments
- **Retraining levy** — a modest tax on the labor cost savings attributable to automation, directed to a national retraining and transition fund for displaced workers — the productivity gain funds the transition
- **AI-generated wealth taxation** — income generated by AI systems is taxed at rates that reflect the public investment in the foundational research, computing infrastructure, and educational institutions that made those systems possible. AI did not emerge from nowhere. It emerged from decades of publicly funded research. The public has a legitimate claim on the returns.

### Why This Matters More Than Any Other Policy Area

AWFixer closes this pillar with an observation that transcends party politics.

Every other policy in this platform — healthcare, criminal justice, anti-corruption, fiscal transparency, labor reform — will eventually be implemented, administered, and enforced through technology. The algorithms that determine benefits eligibility, the systems that flag financial anomalies in the public ledger, the AI tools that assist prosecutors and judges, the platforms through which citizens access government services — these are not neutral infrastructure. They encode values, embed assumptions, and exercise power.

A democratic society that does not understand, control, and remain accountable for the technological systems through which it governs itself is not actually governing itself. It is outsourcing governance to whoever controls the systems — and currently, that is a small number of private companies operating without public accountability, under legal frameworks that criminalize scrutiny, in a political environment that has not yet fully reckoned with what this means.

AWFixer believes this is the foundational challenge of the next generation of American governance. Everything else we propose depends, ultimately, on getting this right.
