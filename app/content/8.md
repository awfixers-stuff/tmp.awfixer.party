# AWFixer Party — Anti-Corruption Policy

---

## PILLAR NINE: Government Corruption — A Comprehensive Reckoning Across Every Branch, Every Level, and Every Uniform

---

### The AWFixer Core Argument

American anti-corruption discourse has a compartmentalization problem. When corruption is discussed in the context of the judiciary, it stays there. When it surfaces in Congress, the conversation is confined to campaign finance and ethics committees that are, almost without exception, staffed by the very people they are supposed to oversee. When it emerges in the executive branch, it becomes a partisan weapon rather than a systemic diagnosis. When it appears in the military, it is quietly absorbed by an institutional culture that has historically treated internal accountability as a threat to unit cohesion and public confidence.

The result is a government in which corruption is addressed nowhere comprehensively because every institution has successfully argued that its corruption is somebody else's problem, or a unique circumstance requiring special handling, or a matter of national security, or simply not as bad as it looks.

AWFixer rejects this entirely.

Corruption is not a judicial problem or a legislative problem or an executive problem or a military problem. It is a **power problem.** Wherever power concentrates without accountability, corruption follows. This is not a controversial observation — it is one of the oldest and most thoroughly documented findings in the entire history of political thought. The American system was designed with this explicitly in mind. The separation of powers, the Bill of Rights, the impeachment mechanism — these are all anti-corruption architecture.

That architecture has been allowed to decay. AWFixer proposes to rebuild it — comprehensively, without institutional carve-outs, and without the pretense that any branch or level of government is too important, too sensitive, or too trusted to require scrutiny.

### The Scale of the Problem — An Honest Inventory

Before proposing solutions, AWFixer insists on naming the problem clearly. The following is not a partisan attack on any administration or party. It is a structural inventory of where corruption has taken root across American governance.

**The Legislative Branch**

Congress has constructed, over decades, a set of ethical standards for itself that would be considered inadequate in most professional fields and illegal in many private sector contexts.

Members of Congress are permitted to trade individual stocks while simultaneously sitting on committees that oversee the industries in which they are trading. The STOCK Act of 2012 was supposed to address this. Its enforcement mechanism — a $200 fine for late disclosure — has proved so toothless that violations are routine and consequence-free. Multiple studies have shown that members of Congress consistently outperform market averages at rates that are statistically difficult to explain without access to non-public information.

The revolving door between congressional staff, members, and the lobbying industry is not merely a conflict of interest — it is the primary mechanism through which legislation is written to serve industry rather than the public. Staff members spend years developing expertise and relationships at public expense, then monetize that expertise and those relationships in the private sector at salaries that make their government compensation look like an internship stipend. The knowledge and access that public service produces are not public property under the current system. They are personal assets to be sold.

Congressional ethics committees are, structurally, a bad joke. Members investigate members. Committees are staffed with an eye toward protecting institutional reputation rather than enforcing standards. The House Ethics Committee has a decades-long history of investigations that begin with fanfare and conclude with nothing. The Senate Ethics Committee is, if anything, less active. The incentive to investigate a colleague is always weaker than the incentive to extend the same professional courtesy you hope to receive when your own conduct is questioned.

**The Executive Branch**

Executive branch corruption operates at two levels that rarely receive equal attention.

The first is the visible, dramatic level — the cabinet official enriching a former employer through a favorable regulatory decision, the political appointee awarding contracts to donors, the department head using public resources for personal benefit. These cases occasionally produce headlines and sometimes produce consequences, though rarely consequences proportionate to the offense.

The second is the structural level, which is both more pervasive and more damaging. The executive branch's regulatory agencies — the FDA, the EPA, the SEC, the FCC, the CFTC, and dozens of others — are systematically staffed with personnel whose professional careers have been, and will again be, tied to the industries they regulate. This is not incidental. It is the predictable result of the fact that the most qualified people to regulate an industry are the people who have worked in it — and those people bring with them loyalties, assumptions, and future employment calculations that inevitably shape regulatory outcomes.

This is regulatory capture, and it is arguably the most consequential form of corruption in the American system because it operates continuously, invisibly, and entirely within the law.

**The Judicial Branch**

Federal judges, including Supreme Court justices, are subject to remarkably limited financial disclosure requirements and essentially no binding ethics code at the Supreme Court level. This situation — which would be considered inadequate for a county clerk in most states — persisted for decades without serious reform, sustained by the institutional mythology that federal judges are simply too distinguished to require the oversight applied to lesser officials.

Recent years have produced extensive documentation of undisclosed gifts, travel, and hospitality received by sitting Supreme Court justices from parties with interests before the Court. The dollar values involved are significant. The failure to disclose is documented. The consequences have been essentially nonexistent.

Below the Supreme Court, federal district and circuit judges face disclosure requirements but enforcement is weak, recusal standards are largely self-administered, and the process by which cases are assigned — and by which certain judges become reliably favorable venues for certain types of litigation — has received insufficient scrutiny.

At the state level, judicial elections in many states have become vehicles for direct investment by interests seeking favorable courts, with campaign contributions flowing from parties who subsequently appear before the judges they helped elect. The Supreme Court's ruling in *Caperton v. Massey* established that extreme cases of this dynamic violate due process, but the threshold is high and the practice continues broadly.

**State and Local Government**

Corruption at the state and local level is arguably more directly harmful to ordinary Americans than federal corruption because it governs the interactions most people actually have with government — building permits, contracts, zoning decisions, law enforcement, school administration, and public works.

The mechanisms are consistent: contracting corruption, where public contracts are awarded through processes that are nominally competitive but practically predetermined; zoning and permitting corruption, where development approvals follow political relationships rather than planning criteria; law enforcement corruption, where departments operate with minimal oversight and internal accountability cultures that treat misconduct as a personnel matter rather than a public safety crisis; and electoral corruption, where the combination of weak campaign finance enforcement and concentrated local political power creates conditions for systematic manipulation.

The federal government's capacity to address state and local corruption is limited by constitutional structure and by the practical reality that federal prosecution resources are finite. AWFixer's approach addresses this at the structural level rather than relying entirely on federal enforcement.

**The Military — A Special and Serious Reckoning**

The military occupies a unique position in American culture — one that has, for understandable historical reasons, been treated as largely exempt from the public scrutiny applied to civilian institutions. AWFixer respects the service and sacrifice of military personnel at every level. That respect is precisely why we believe the military deserves better accountability than it currently receives.

The documented problems are serious and have been insufficiently addressed:

*Financial accountability* is perhaps the starkest failure. As noted in the fiscal transparency pillar, the Department of Defense has never passed a comprehensive audit. Ever. The DoD's budget — currently over $850 billion annually — is the largest discretionary expenditure in the federal government, and it has operated for decades without the basic financial controls that any private company of comparable size would be legally required to maintain. This is not a technical accounting problem. It is a structural accountability failure of the first order.

*Procurement corruption* in the defense industry represents one of the most durable and expensive forms of corruption in American government. The revolving door between Pentagon leadership and defense contractors is institutionalized to a degree that makes the civilian regulatory revolving door look modest. Generals and admirals retire and immediately assume board positions and senior advisory roles at the companies whose contracts they helped award. Cost overruns of hundreds of percent on major weapons systems are routine and consequence-free. The F-35 program — the most expensive weapons acquisition in human history — has cost over $400 billion and counting, has missed performance specifications consistently, and has produced accountability for no one at any level.

*Sexual assault and harassment* within the military represents a documented, systemic failure that has persisted despite decades of official acknowledgment and promised reform. The Military Justice Improvement Act and its successors have attempted to address the fundamental structural problem — that sexual assault cases are investigated and prosecuted within the same chain of command that the accused typically serves in — but implementation has been slow, resistance from within the institutional culture has been substantial, and survivor outcomes remain deeply inadequate. AWFixer treats this not as a cultural problem to be gradually improved but as a justice failure requiring structural remedy.

*Hazing, abuse, and toxic command culture* have produced documented patterns of harm to service members that are inadequately investigated and rarely result in meaningful accountability. The deaths of soldiers like Vanessa Guillén — whose 2020 murder at Fort Hood exposed a pattern of harassment complaints that were systematically ignored — represent the visible tip of a much larger problem of internal complaint suppression within military commands.

*Contractor abuse overseas* — including documented cases involving private military contractors operating under DoD contracts — has created accountability vacuums where serious misconduct, up to and including homicide, has occurred in environments where neither military justice nor civilian law applies effectively.

AWFixer does not raise these issues to undermine the military or diminish the genuine service of the overwhelming majority of personnel who serve honorably. We raise them because a military that cannot hold itself accountable is not only failing its own members — it is accumulating the kind of institutional rot that, historically, has preceded far more serious failures.

### The AWFixer Anti-Corruption Architecture

---

### INSTITUTION ONE: The Independent Anti-Corruption Commission (IACC)

The centerpiece of AWFixer's anti-corruption policy is the creation of a genuinely independent federal anti-corruption body — not an inspector general embedded within the agencies it oversees, not an ethics committee staffed by the institution's own members, but a structurally independent commission with jurisdiction across all three branches of the federal government and investigative authority over state and local corruption involving federal funds.

**Structure:**

The IACC is modeled on independent anti-corruption bodies that have demonstrated effectiveness in comparable democracies — notably the Independent Commission Against Corruption in Hong Kong, which in two decades transformed one of the world's most corrupt governments into one of its cleaner ones, and similar bodies in Australia and Singapore.

- **Seven commissioners** appointed through a process deliberately designed to prevent single-party or single-branch control: two appointed by the President, two by congressional leadership of each party, two by a panel of federal appellate judges selected by lot, and one by a civic selection panel drawn from nonpartisan civil society organizations
- **Fixed eight-year terms**, non-renewable, staggered to prevent simultaneous replacement
- **Removal only by supermajority** of the full commission plus independent judicial review — not removable by the appointing authority
- **Dedicated budget** constitutionally protected from legislative reduction below a defined floor — preventing the defunding that has historically been used to neutralize oversight bodies

**Jurisdiction:**

- All three branches of the federal government including, explicitly, the Supreme Court and the military
- State and local officials in matters involving federal funds or federal constitutional violations
- Private contractors, lobbyists, and other non-governmental actors engaged in corrupt relationships with government officials
- Former officials for conduct during their government service, with no statute of limitations for conduct that was actively concealed

**Powers:**

- Subpoena authority over documents, financial records, and testimony
- Independent prosecution authority — the IACC can bring cases without routing through the Department of Justice, eliminating the conflict of interest inherent in asking the executive branch to prosecute itself
- Asset freezing authority pending investigation of financial corruption
- Whistleblower protection and management — the IACC is the designated safe harbor for government whistleblowers, with the authority to investigate retaliation independently of the agency involved
- Public reporting — the IACC publishes detailed public reports on all closed investigations, including those that did not result in prosecution, with findings of fact that inform public accountability even when criminal prosecution is not warranted

### INSTITUTION TWO: Comprehensive Ethics Reform by Branch

**Congress:**

- **Mandatory stock divestiture.** Members of Congress, upon taking office, divest individual stock holdings into blind trusts or index funds. This is not a radical proposal — it is the standard applied to executive branch cabinet officials. There is no coherent argument for why it should not apply equally to legislators.
- **Lifetime lobbying ban** for members; five-year ban for senior staff. The revolving door is not a perk of public service. The relationships and knowledge developed at public expense are public assets.
- **Real STOCK Act enforcement.** The $200 fine for disclosure violations is replaced with penalties scaled to the value of the undisclosed trades, mandatory disgorgement of profits from trades made during periods of relevant committee service, and criminal referral for patterns of violation.
- **Independent ethics enforcement.** The House and Senate ethics committees are replaced with an independent Congressional Ethics Authority staffed by retired federal judges, former prosecutors, and civic appointees — none of whom are current or former members of Congress.
- **Earmark transparency.** Every earmark, budget rider, and spending directive attached to legislation must be publicly attributed to the sponsoring member on the public ledger described in Pillar Seven, at the time of insertion — not disclosed months later in appendices that no one reads.

**The Executive Branch:**

- **Mandatory cooling-off periods** of five years before any executive branch official or senior career employee can work in an industry they regulated, advised on, or contracted with during their government service
- **Contractor disclosure** — all contracts awarded to firms that have employed former officials of the awarding agency within the previous ten years must be disclosed with that relationship explicitly flagged on the public ledger
- **Political appointee financial disclosure** at the same level currently required of judges — full asset disclosure, spousal income disclosure, and ongoing annual updates
- **Regulatory capture audit** — the IACC conducts a biennial audit of the personnel composition of major regulatory agencies, tracking the proportion of leadership drawn from regulated industries and publishing findings publicly

**The Judicial Branch:**

- **Binding Supreme Court ethics code.** The Supreme Court is not exempt from the ethics standards applied to lower federal courts. AWFixer supports legislation establishing a binding code of conduct for Supreme Court justices with enforcement through the IACC rather than through self-administration
- **Mandatory recusal standards** with independent review — a justice or judge who declines to recuse despite a disclosed relationship with a party must provide a written public explanation, which is subject to IACC review
- **Full financial disclosure** for all federal judges including gifts, travel, hospitality, and speaking fees, with the same disclosure standards applied to executive branch officials
- **Judicial appointment transparency** — the process by which federal judges are recommended, vetted, and nominated should be documented and publicly visible, with explicit disclosure of any communication between nominees and senators or White House officials outside of formal hearings

### INSTITUTION THREE: Military Accountability Reform

AWFixer treats military accountability as a separate and serious category requiring dedicated structural remedies rather than the continued promise of cultural change that has characterized thirty years of inadequate reform.

**Financial Accountability:**

- **Mandatory DoD audit completion within four years**, with the IACC empowered to investigate impediments to audit completion as potential obstruction
- **Criminal referral** for financial officers who certify accounts they know to be inaccurate — the same standard applied to corporate executives under Sarbanes-Oxley applied to defense financial management
- **Procurement reform** with independent cost estimation — major weapons programs require cost and performance estimates from bodies independent of the program office and independent of the contractors bidding on the work, with IACC review of programs that exceed estimates by more than 15%
- **Revolving door restrictions** identical to those applied to civilian agencies — retired generals and admirals are subject to a five-year cooling-off period before employment with defense contractors whose contracts they influenced

**Military Justice Reform:**

- **Complete removal of sexual assault prosecution** from the chain of command — cases are investigated and prosecuted by an independent military prosecutor's office that reports to the IACC rather than to the service branch command structure. This is the structural reform that has been resisted for decades and that survivor advocates have consistently identified as the essential change
- **Independent investigation of harassment and abuse complaints** — complaints filed against a service member's direct chain of command are automatically routed to an independent investigative body rather than processed within the same command
- **Whistleblower protection for service members** with the same legal force as civilian whistleblower protections — a service member who reports misconduct cannot face adverse action, reassignment, or informal retaliation without triggering IACC investigation
- **Mandatory public reporting** of complaint outcomes — the number of complaints filed, investigated, prosecuted, and resolved by type, branch, and command level, published annually with sufficient granularity to identify systemic patterns

**Contractor Accountability:**

- **Extended jurisdiction** for serious crimes committed by American contractors overseas under DoD contracts — the legal vacuum that has allowed contractor misconduct to go unprosecuted is closed through explicit statutory jurisdiction in federal courts
- **Contractor debarment** for firms whose employees commit serious offenses overseas — the company is accountable for the conduct it enables
- **IACC oversight** of contractor conduct investigations currently handled internally by DoD — the same conflict of interest that corrupts military justice internally applies to contractor investigations

### INSTITUTION FOUR: State and Local Anti-Corruption

Federal authority over state and local corruption is constitutionally limited, but AWFixer believes federal leverage — particularly through funding conditions — can drive meaningful structural reform without overriding state sovereignty.

- **Funding conditionality** — states and municipalities receiving federal funds above a defined threshold are required to maintain independent ethics bodies meeting federal minimum standards as a condition of funding. This is the same mechanism used to drive adoption of drinking age laws, environmental standards, and highway safety requirements. It works.
- **Federal prosecution resources** for public corruption are dramatically increased — the FBI's Public Corruption Unit and the DOJ's Public Integrity Section are chronically understaffed relative to the scope of the problem. AWFixer supports tripling their budgets and staffing
- **State constitutional reform support** — the federal government provides technical assistance and model legislation to states seeking to establish independent anti-corruption bodies at the state level, based on the IACC model
- **Judicial election reform** — federal funding incentives for states to move toward merit selection of judges rather than contested elections funded by interested parties

### PRINCIPLE: Corruption Is Bipartisan and the Response Must Be Too

AWFixer states this clearly because it must be said clearly: **corruption is not a Republican problem or a Democratic problem.** It is a power problem, and both parties have demonstrated — extensively and with rich documentary evidence — that they are equally capable of it when the structural incentives permit.

An anti-corruption architecture that is perceived as partisan will be dismantled by the next administration of the opposing party. The structural design of the IACC — specifically the multipartisan appointment process and the supermajority removal requirement — is intended to make it politically costly to attack regardless of which party holds power.

This also means AWFixer applies this framework without selective application. The same standards apply to a Democratic senator trading on committee knowledge and a Republican contractor overbilling on a defense contract. The same standards apply to a liberal judge failing to disclose gifts and a conservative one. The same standards apply to a general who covered up assault allegations under a Democratic administration and one who did so under a Republican one.

If AWFixer ever finds itself applying this framework selectively — prosecuting the other side's corruption while protecting its own — it will have become exactly what it set out to dismantle. We consider that a founding commitment, not a campaign slogan.
