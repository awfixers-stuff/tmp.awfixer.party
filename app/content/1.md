**Welcome to the AWFixer Party**

The AWFixer Political Party is a new political movement built on honest governance, economic reform, and a willingness to say what others won't. Our core values aren't talking points — they're commitments.

**Core Values**

**1. Deregulation and Competition Drive Down Prices**
Monopolies and regulatory capture don't protect consumers — they protect incumbents. When industries are shielded from competition by red tape, prices rise and quality falls. We believe that smart deregulation, paired with strong antitrust enforcement, forces businesses to compete for customers rather than lobby for protection. The result is lower prices, more innovation, and a marketplace that actually works for ordinary Americans.

**2. Pro-Business Means More Tax Revenue**
Supporting business doesn't mean cutting taxes into oblivion — it means creating the conditions where businesses *thrive and pay their share*. A healthy, growing economy with a broad tax base generates far more public revenue than a system riddled with loopholes built for the well-connected. We are pro-business *and* pro-accountability. Those aren't opposites.

**3. Intellectual and Moral Honesty in Leadership**
Too many political parties govern by poll numbers. We believe leadership requires the courage to be right even when it's unpopular — and the humility to admit when you're wrong. We hold ourselves to a higher standard of reasoning and ethical consistency, and we expect our representatives to do the same.

**How America Lost Its Way: 30 Years of Regression**

Since Ronald Reagan left office in 1989, the United States has quietly drifted back toward the conditions that defined the pre-WWI Gilded Age — concentrated wealth, weakened labor, and government that serves industry over citizens. The following examples illustrate this pattern.

---

**Case Study 1: The Nixon Administration and the Betrayal of Energy Independence**

Though predating Reagan, the Nixon era offers a foundational example of America's two-faced approach to governance — promising one thing publicly while doing another structurally.

In 1973, in response to the OPEC oil embargo, Nixon launched **Project Independence** — an ambitious national goal to achieve full U.S. energy self-sufficiency by 1980. The rhetoric was bold: America would never again be held hostage to foreign oil.

Then came the **Nuclear Regulatory Commission (NRC)**, established in 1975. While framed as a consumer safety reform (splitting the old Atomic Energy Commission into a promoter and a regulator), the NRC's strict and slow licensing process effectively strangled the nuclear energy expansion that Project Independence depended on. Decades of permitting delays followed, nuclear development stalled, and American energy dependence on foreign sources *deepened* rather than shrinking.

The lesson: America announced a vision and then built the institutional machinery to prevent it from happening. This pattern — bold public goals paired with quiet structural sabotage — is one we've seen repeated ever since.
