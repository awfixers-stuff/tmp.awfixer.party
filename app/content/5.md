## PILLAR SIX: Energy Policy — Nuclear Deregulation and the Honest Environmental Argument

---

### The AWFixer Energy Doctrine

America's energy debate has been trapped for decades in a false choice between economic growth and environmental responsibility, between fossil fuels and unreliable renewables, between energy independence and environmental conscience. AWFixer rejects this framing entirely.

There is an energy source that is:
- **Carbon-free** in operation
- **Reliable** regardless of weather, season, or time of day
- **Scalable** to meet industrial, residential, and transportation demand simultaneously
- **Land-efficient** to a degree no other clean energy source approaches
- **Proven** over seven decades of operation across dozens of countries

That source is nuclear power. And America has been systematically dismantling its capacity to use it for fifty years.

This is not an accident. It is the result of regulatory capture, institutional inertia, fossil fuel industry influence, and a public fear campaign built on misunderstood science. AWFixer intends to name all of that clearly and reverse course.

---

### The Historical Failure — Revisiting Project Independence

As noted in our founding case studies, this story begins with Nixon's Project Independence in 1973 and the Nuclear Regulatory Commission that quietly strangled it. That history is worth dwelling on before we talk about solutions, because the mechanism of failure then is the same mechanism we must dismantle now.

The NRC was created in 1975 with a legitimate purpose — separating the promotional and regulatory functions of nuclear oversight that had been uncomfortably combined in the old Atomic Energy Commission. No serious person objects to independent safety regulation of nuclear facilities. That is reasonable and necessary.

What happened next was not reasonable or necessary.

The NRC's licensing process became one of the most expensive, time-consuming, and unpredictable regulatory gauntlets in the entire American economy. A nuclear plant that might take five years to build in France or South Korea takes fifteen to twenty years to license and build in the United States — if it gets built at all. Licensing applications run to millions of pages. Designs certified as safe can be challenged mid-construction on grounds that were available at the start. Cost overruns driven by regulatory delay become arguments against nuclear's economic viability — conveniently ignoring that the regulatory process itself created the cost overruns.

Meanwhile, the fossil fuel industry spent lavishly to ensure that nuclear's primary competitor for baseload power — natural gas — faced no comparable regulatory burden. The result was entirely predictable: American nuclear capacity stagnated, gas expanded, and the country that pioneered commercial nuclear power now builds essentially none of it while France generates 70% of its electricity from nuclear and pays some of the lowest electricity prices in Europe.

We built the cage. We locked ourselves in it. AWFixer intends to open the door.

### The Honest Environmental Argument

AWFixer is willing to say something that most pro-environment politicians are not: **not all clean energy is created equal, and pretending otherwise is intellectually dishonest.**

The dominant political narrative treats solar, wind, and hydroelectric power as unambiguous environmental goods and nuclear as a dangerous legacy technology to be phased out. The evidence does not support this narrative.

**Solar Fields: The Hidden Footprint**

Utility-scale solar generation requires enormous amounts of land. To generate the same amount of electricity that a single nuclear plant produces, a solar installation requires roughly **75 times the land area.** That land, typically in desert or semi-arid ecosystems, is not empty. It supports plant communities, reptile populations, migratory bird corridors, and soil ecosystems that took centuries to develop. Large-scale solar development in the American Southwest has already come into direct conflict with tortoise habitats, native plant communities, and the open landscapes that define those environments.

Solar panels also require mining. Significant quantities of silicon, silver, indium, tellurium, and rare earth elements go into panel manufacturing — mining operations that carry their own environmental costs, largely externalized to the countries where the extraction occurs. Panels have a lifespan of 25–30 years, after which they become a rapidly growing waste stream that current recycling infrastructure is not equipped to handle at scale.

None of this means solar is bad. Rooftop solar on existing structures is genuinely low-impact and AWFixer supports its expansion aggressively. What we are saying is that utility-scale solar fields are not the environmental free lunch they are often presented as. They are a significant land use choice with real ecological consequences that deserve honest accounting.

**Hydroelectric Dams: Proven Ecological Destruction**

The environmental case against large-scale hydroelectric power is, if anything, stronger and more thoroughly documented than the case against solar fields — yet it receives far less political attention because hydro has been grandfathered into the "clean energy" category without scrutiny.

Large dams do the following, consistently and measurably:

- **Destroy river ecosystems.** The reservoir behind a dam floods the river valley, eliminating the riparian habitat that supports the greatest concentration of biodiversity of any terrestrial environment. The Snake River dams in the Pacific Northwest have driven multiple salmon runs to the edge of extinction. The Three Gorges Dam in China displaced over a million people and eliminated the habitat of the Yangtze river dolphin, which is now functionally extinct.

- **Fragment fish migration.** Even with fish ladders, dams are barriers to the migratory patterns that anadromous fish — salmon, steelhead, shad, sturgeon — have evolved over millions of years. Fish ladders help. They do not solve the problem.

- **Alter downstream hydrology permanently.** Dams trap sediment that would otherwise nourish river deltas and coastal wetlands. The Colorado River, one of the most dammed in the world, no longer reaches the sea. The delta that once supported one of the most productive estuaries in North America is now a dry salt flat.

- **Release methane.** Reservoirs in tropical and subtropical regions produce significant methane emissions from decomposing organic matter — a greenhouse gas far more potent than CO₂ over short timeframes. Large hydroelectric projects in some climates produce more greenhouse gas per kilowatt-hour than natural gas plants.

The environmental movement has largely given hydroelectric a pass because it predates modern ecological awareness and because it is renewable in a narrow technical sense. AWFixer believes this inconsistency undermines the credibility of energy policy debates. If we are serious about environmental impact, we must be consistent about measuring it.

**Nuclear: The Environmental Case For**

Against this honest accounting of solar and hydro, nuclear's environmental profile looks remarkably strong:

- **Land use:** A nuclear plant producing 1,000 megawatts occupies roughly one square mile including all facilities and exclusion zones. The equivalent solar installation would require 75 square miles. The equivalent wind installation would require over 300 square miles.

- **Carbon emissions:** Nuclear power's lifecycle carbon emissions — including mining, construction, operation, and decommissioning — are comparable to wind and lower than solar on a per-kilowatt-hour basis. They are roughly 70 times lower than natural gas and 100 times lower than coal.

- **Waste:** Nuclear waste is real, it is dangerous, and it must be managed responsibly. It is also remarkably small in volume. All of the spent nuclear fuel ever produced by American commercial reactors would fit within an area the size of a football field stacked roughly ten yards deep. It is solid, it does not disperse, and it can be isolated. Compare this to the waste stream of fossil fuel combustion, which is released directly into the atmosphere in quantities measured in billions of tons annually. The nuclear waste problem is serious and solvable. The fossil fuel waste problem is civilization-scale and currently unaddressed.

- **Wildlife:** Nuclear plants occupy small footprints and their exclusion zones — ironically — often become de facto wildlife refuges. The exclusion zone around Chernobyl, despite the accident, has become one of the most wildlife-rich areas in Europe simply because humans have not been present for forty years. Nuclear power, operated correctly, is one of the least ecologically disruptive large-scale energy sources that exists.

### The Safety Argument — Confronting Fear With Facts

No serious energy policy conversation about nuclear can avoid the safety question. AWFixer addresses it directly.

**The statistical reality is this:** nuclear power has the lowest deaths-per-unit-of-energy-produced of any power source in the world, including wind and solar. This is not a pro-nuclear talking point. It is the consistent finding of peer-reviewed energy safety research across multiple independent institutions.

Coal kills roughly 25 people per terawatt-hour of electricity produced, primarily through air pollution. Natural gas kills about 3. Wind kills about 0.04 — mostly from installation and maintenance accidents. Nuclear kills about **0.03**, a figure that *includes* the deaths attributable to Chernobyl and Fukushima.

Chernobyl was a catastrophic accident involving a reactor design that Western nations never used, operated by a government that actively suppressed safety concerns and delayed evacuation. It killed fewer people than coal kills every week. Fukushima, caused by a 9.0 magnitude earthquake and the largest tsunami in recorded Japanese history, killed one person from radiation. One. The evacuations that followed — which AWFixer acknowledges were driven by understandable public fear — killed hundreds through the stress and disruption of displacement.

The public fear of nuclear power is not irrational — it is a predictable response to vivid, dramatic events amplified by decades of cultural messaging from films, books, and advocacy. But policy should not be driven by availability bias. It should be driven by evidence. And the evidence is unambiguous: nuclear power is extraordinarily safe, and treating it as uniquely dangerous while burning millions of tons of coal and gas annually is a policy choice that kills people.

### The AWFixer Nuclear Deregulation Program

**1. Overhaul the NRC Licensing Process**

The current NRC licensing timeline is incompatible with building new nuclear capacity at the speed climate goals require. AWFixer supports:

- Establishing a **hard timeline** for licensing decisions — applications must receive a final determination within three years of submission, with clear criteria for approval, denial, or required modification
- Moving to **design certification rather than site-by-site review** for approved reactor designs. If a reactor design has been certified as safe, individual plants using that design should require substantially less review than a novel first-of-kind installation
- **Streamlining the appeal and intervention process** to prevent regulatory delay from being weaponized by opponents who have exhausted legitimate technical objections but continue to litigate process
- Maintaining rigorous safety standards throughout — deregulation here means removing bureaucratic obstruction, not removing safety requirements

**2. Embrace Advanced and Small Modular Reactor Technology**

The nuclear industry of 2026 is not the nuclear industry of 1975. A new generation of reactor designs addresses many of the concerns — cost, safety, waste, scale — that have been used to argue against nuclear expansion:

- **Small Modular Reactors (SMRs)** can be factory-manufactured and deployed at a fraction of the cost and construction time of traditional large-scale plants. They can be scaled to match community energy needs, sited on smaller footprints, and deployed in regions where a full-scale nuclear plant would be impractical
- **Generation IV reactor designs** — including molten salt, high-temperature gas, and fast neutron reactors — offer passive safety systems that physically cannot produce a Chernobyl-style runaway reaction. Some designs can consume existing spent fuel waste as part of their fuel cycle, addressing the waste problem and the fuel supply simultaneously
- **Fusion research** deserves sustained federal investment. Practical fusion power remains a long-term goal but recent advances in private fusion development suggest the timeline may be shorter than conventional wisdom assumed. America should be leading this race, not watching it

**3. Phase Out Oil and Gas on a Defined Timeline**

AWFixer believes in honesty. An honest energy transition acknowledges several things simultaneously:

- Oil and gas cannot be eliminated overnight without catastrophic economic disruption — the infrastructure, supply chains, and industrial processes that depend on fossil fuels cannot be replaced faster than alternatives can be built
- A defined, credible phase-out timeline is essential — without it, the fossil fuel industry will continue to make long-term investments that lock in decades of additional carbon emissions, and the political will to transition will continue to dissipate
- The fastest path away from fossil fuels is through nuclear baseload power, which can replace gas-fired electricity generation at scale and reliability that no current renewable combination can match
- Workers in fossil fuel industries deserve transition support — retraining programs, economic development investment in affected communities, and honest acknowledgment that this transition will require adjustment

**AWFixer proposed timeline:**
- **Years 1–5:** Expedite NRC reform; fast-track SMR deployment approvals; begin phase-out of coal-fired generation with nuclear replacement priority
- **Years 5–15:** Scale SMR deployment nationally; begin systematic replacement of gas-fired baseload with nuclear; expand rooftop solar and storage as distributed complements
- **Years 15–30:** Complete coal and oil phase-out from electricity generation; accelerate industrial and transportation electrification from a nuclear-dominated grid; reassess natural gas role in hard-to-electrify industrial processes

**4. Invest in a National Nuclear Workforce**

America's nuclear engineering and skilled trades pipeline has atrophied alongside the industry itself. Rebuilding it requires:

- Federal investment in nuclear engineering programs at universities and technical colleges
- Apprenticeship and certification programs for the construction trades that build and maintain nuclear facilities
- Competitive compensation frameworks that make nuclear careers attractive relative to the oil and gas sector, which has historically offered strong wages

### Why This Is the Environmentally Serious Position

AWFixer anticipates that this platform will draw criticism from environmental advocates who have built their identity around opposition to nuclear power. We respect that advocacy tradition while disagreeing with its conclusions on this specific point.

The measure of an environmental policy is not how it feels or what it signals — it is whether it results in less damage to the natural systems that human and non-human life depend on. On that measure, the continued opposition to nuclear power while simultaneously opposing fossil fuels has contributed to an impossible policy gridlock that has benefited one actor above all others: the fossil fuel industry.

Every year that passes without new nuclear capacity is a year filled with natural gas. Every nuclear plant that closes is replaced, in practice, not by wind and solar but by gas peaker plants that run whenever the sun isn't shining and the wind isn't blowing. Germany's celebrated *Energiewende* — the aggressive shutdown of nuclear in favor of renewables — resulted in Germany burning more coal and paying higher electricity prices while its carbon emissions *increased* in the short term.

The planet does not care about ideological consistency. It cares about how many tons of carbon are being released and how many acres of habitat are being destroyed. On both measures, nuclear is the most defensible large-scale energy choice available to us right now, with the technology we actually have, at the scale the problem actually requires.

That is AWFixer's position. We are prepared to defend it.
