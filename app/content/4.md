# AWFixer Party — Labor & Immigration Policy

---

## PILLAR FIVE: Labor & Immigration — Fix the Incentives, Not the People

---

### The AWFixer Core Insight

Most American political parties treat labor and immigration as separate issues requiring separate solutions. AWFixer recognizes them as **the same problem wearing different hats.**

The immigration debate in America has been almost entirely focused on the supply side — how many people are coming, how to stop them, how to find and remove those already here. This is the wrong frame entirely. It is expensive, ineffective, and inhumane. It also deliberately avoids the more uncomfortable question:

**Why are they being hired?**

Because someone is hiring them. Consistently. At scale. For decades. And that someone is making a business decision that American labor policy has made not only possible, but *economically rational.*

AWFixer believes that if you remove the economic incentive to hire foreign labor over American labor, you solve the immigration problem without militarizing the border, without mass deportation operations, and without tearing apart communities. You also solve significant chunks of the labor problem at the same time — because the same forces that suppress wages through outsourcing and undocumented labor also suppress wages through offshoring, automation incentives, and union erosion.

Fix the incentive structure. The behavior follows.

### PRINCIPLE ONE: Remove the Incentive to Hire Foreign Labor

**The Problem:**

American businesses hire undocumented workers and offshore American jobs for the same fundamental reason — it's cheaper. Not because American workers are unwilling to do the work, but because the system has been structured, deliberately and over time, to make exploitable labor more accessible and less accountable than fairly compensated domestic labor.

The enforcement model — raids, deportations, border walls — attacks the symptom while leaving the disease entirely intact. The day after a workplace raid, the economic incentive to hire cheap, unprotected labor still exists. Someone will fill that gap.

**AWFixer Policy Positions:**

- **Employer accountability over worker punishment.** Shift the enforcement focus dramatically toward the businesses and individuals doing the hiring. Meaningful, consistently enforced penalties for hiring undocumented workers — not token fines that are priced in as a cost of doing business, but penalties scaled to the size and profitability of the employer — change the calculation at the source.

- **Close the outsourcing subsidy.** American tax law currently allows corporations to defer taxes on profits earned abroad and to deduct the costs of moving operations overseas. This means American taxpayers are effectively subsidizing the export of American jobs. AWFixer supports eliminating these deductions and closing offshore profit-sheltering mechanisms so that outsourcing reflects its true cost rather than an artificially subsidized one.

- **Level the playing field on labor costs.** When an American company outsources manufacturing to a country with no environmental standards, no worker safety requirements, and wages held artificially low by authoritarian suppression, they aren't competing — they're arbitraging a human rights deficit. AWFixer supports import standards that require goods entering the American market to meet baseline labor and environmental benchmarks, creating a floor that genuine competition can operate above.

- **Simplify and accelerate legal work authorization.** Part of what drives undocumented labor is that the legal pathway is slow, expensive, and dysfunctional. Employers who genuinely need specialized foreign labor should have a fast, transparent, and accountable process for obtaining it — one that prevents abuse rather than incentivizing workarounds. A functioning legal system reduces the pressure that drives the illegal one.

- **Pathways, not purges.** For the estimated 10–12 million undocumented people already living and working in the United States, AWFixer does not support mass deportation. It is logistically impossible, fiscally ruinous, and morally untenable. We support a structured pathway to legal status — not amnesty without conditions, but a defined process involving back taxes, registration, background checks, and demonstrated community ties. People who have built lives here are not the problem. The system that made their situation economically inevitable is.

### PRINCIPLE TWO: Reforming Labor Unions Through Independent Oversight

This is a position that will make some enemies. AWFixer accepts that.

**The Honest Assessment:**

Labor unions were one of the most important institutions in the development of the American middle class. The 40-hour work week, the weekend, workplace safety standards, employer-provided healthcare, the end of child labor in industry — these did not come from the goodwill of corporations. They came from organized labor, often at significant personal cost to the workers who fought for them.

That history deserves respect. What it does not deserve is a free pass on the present.

American labor unions today are a mixed picture. Some remain effective advocates for their members. Many have become something closer to political fundraising machines with a labor branding problem. Decades of lobbying Congress — the same Congress that regulates, funds, and oversees labor law — has produced a cozy interdependency that serves union leadership and political donors while delivering diminishing returns for rank-and-file workers.

Union membership in the private sector has declined from roughly 35% of the workforce in the 1950s to under 6% today. This is not entirely the fault of unions — aggressive corporate union-busting, legislative erosion of organizing rights, and the structural shift away from manufacturing have all played a role. But the unions themselves have not been blameless. Corruption, leadership entrenchment, and the prioritization of political influence over member outcomes have damaged credibility and weakened organizing efforts at exactly the moments when workers needed them most.

It is intellectually dishonest to look at this trajectory and declare the current structure sound.

**AWFixer Policy Positions:**

- **Establish an Independent Labor Union Oversight Board (ILUOB).** This body would be structured similarly to an independent judiciary — appointed through a nonpartisan process, insulated from congressional lobbying pressure, with fixed terms and conflict-of-interest requirements. Its mandate would be to:
    - Audit union finances and ensure member dues are being spent in members' interests
    - Investigate and prosecute union corruption without political interference
    - Ensure democratic governance within unions — secret ballots, transparent leadership elections, member access to financial records
    - Mediate disputes between union leadership and membership when the two are demonstrably misaligned

- **Separate union political activity from union labor activity.** Unions should be extraordinarily effective at negotiating wages, benefits, working conditions, and job security. Political donations and lobbying activity should be strictly separated from dues-funded operations and require explicit, individual member opt-in. A worker who wants union representation should not be required to fund political positions they may not share.

- **Restore and modernize organizing rights.** The decline of private sector union membership has not been a neutral market outcome. It has been the result of decades of legislative and legal erosion of the right to organize. AWFixer supports restoring meaningful organizing protections — including stronger penalties for illegal retaliation against organizing workers and streamlined election processes — while pairing those restored rights with the accountability structures described above. Stronger unions that are genuinely accountable to their members are good for workers, and ultimately good for the economy.

- **Extend oversight to public sector unions as well.** The conflict of interest in public sector union negotiations — where unions bargain against elected officials whose campaigns they helped fund — is a structural problem that neither party has been willing to address honestly. AWFixer supports the same oversight framework applying to public sector unions, with specific attention to the pension and benefit commitments that have created long-term fiscal crises in numerous states and municipalities.

### PRINCIPLE THREE: Civil Standard of Living Over Minimum Wage

This is one of the most genuinely original positions in the AWFixer platform, and it deserves careful explanation.

**The Problem With Minimum Wage:**

The minimum wage debate is one of the most reliably unproductive arguments in American politics. One side picks a number and calls it justice. The other side picks the same number and calls it a job-killer. Neither side is entirely wrong, and neither side is solving the actual problem.

The minimum wage is a blunt instrument applied to a complex system. A dollar figure set in Washington means something very different in rural Mississippi than it does in San Francisco. It also measures only one dimension — hourly cash wages — while ignoring the full picture of what it actually costs to live a dignified human life in America today.

**The AWFixer Alternative: The Civil Standard of Living (CSL)**

Instead of mandating a minimum hourly wage, AWFixer proposes the development and maintenance of a **Civil Standard of Living Index** — a regularly updated, regionally adjusted measure of what it actually costs to meet a baseline of dignified existence in America.

The CSL would account for:
- Housing (rent or mortgage at non-exploitative market rates for the region)
- Nutritious food
- Healthcare, including preventive care and mental health
- Transportation (including non-car options where relevant)
- Childcare and dependent care
- Utilities including broadband internet, which is now an employment prerequisite
- A modest discretionary component — because a life with no capacity for leisure, culture, or savings is not a dignified one

**Why This Works Better:**

Rather than mandating a dollar figure, the CSL creates a **publicly visible benchmark** against which compensation packages — wages plus benefits plus non-cash compensation — can be measured. The policy mechanisms AWFixer would attach to this benchmark are designed to make meeting it the path of least resistance for businesses, rather than the legally required floor:

- **Tax incentives for CSL-compliant employers.** Businesses that demonstrably compensate their full-time workforce at or above the regional CSL — counting wages, healthcare contributions, retirement matching, and other material benefits — receive favorable tax treatment. The government isn't dictating pay; it's making fair pay the smarter financial decision.

- **Public procurement preferences.** Federal and state contracting would favor CSL-compliant employers. If you want government business, you take care of the people doing the work.

- **Transparency requirements.** Large employers would be required to publish their median total compensation relative to the regional CSL, creating public accountability and reputational incentives without mandating specific dollar figures.

- **Regional calibration.** Because the CSL is indexed to actual cost of living by region, it avoids the bluntness of a single national number. A small business in rural Alabama and a tech firm in Seattle are operating in genuinely different economic environments. The CSL acknowledges that reality instead of pretending it doesn't exist.

- **Regular updates.** The CSL would be recalculated annually by an independent economic body — not a politically appointed committee with an agenda, but career economists using standardized methodology. This removes the political theater of minimum wage fights from the legislative calendar.

**The Philosophical Difference:**

Minimum wage sets a floor and dares businesses to stay above it. The Civil Standard of Living defines what a human life in America actually requires, makes that information publicly available and economically relevant, and creates conditions where meeting it is the rational business choice. It treats employers as capable of making good decisions when the incentive structure supports those decisions — which is, notably, the same principle AWFixer applies everywhere else in the platform.
