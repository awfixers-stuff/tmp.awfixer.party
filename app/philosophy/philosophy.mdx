## This is a draft of our philosophy

**Welcome to the AWFixer Party**

The AWFixer Political Party is a new political movement built on honest governance, economic reform, and a willingness to say what others won't. Our core values aren't talking points — they're commitments.

---

**Core Values**

**1. Deregulation and Competition Drive Down Prices**
Monopolies and regulatory capture don't protect consumers — they protect incumbents. When industries are shielded from competition by red tape, prices rise and quality falls. We believe that smart deregulation, paired with strong antitrust enforcement, forces businesses to compete for customers rather than lobby for protection. The result is lower prices, more innovation, and a marketplace that actually works for ordinary Americans.

**2. Pro-Business Means More Tax Revenue**
Supporting business doesn't mean cutting taxes into oblivion — it means creating the conditions where businesses *thrive and pay their share*. A healthy, growing economy with a broad tax base generates far more public revenue than a system riddled with loopholes built for the well-connected. We are pro-business *and* pro-accountability. Those aren't opposites.

**3. Intellectual and Moral Honesty in Leadership**
Too many political parties govern by poll numbers. We believe leadership requires the courage to be right even when it's unpopular — and the humility to admit when you're wrong. We hold ourselves to a higher standard of reasoning and ethical consistency, and we expect our representatives to do the same.

---

**How America Lost Its Way: 30 Years of Regression**

Since Ronald Reagan left office in 1989, the United States has quietly drifted back toward the conditions that defined the pre-WWI Gilded Age — concentrated wealth, weakened labor, and government that serves industry over citizens. The following examples illustrate this pattern.

---

**Case Study 1: The Nixon Administration and the Betrayal of Energy Independence**

Though predating Reagan, the Nixon era offers a foundational example of America's two-faced approach to governance — promising one thing publicly while doing another structurally.

In 1973, in response to the OPEC oil embargo, Nixon launched **Project Independence** — an ambitious national goal to achieve full U.S. energy self-sufficiency by 1980. The rhetoric was bold: America would never again be held hostage to foreign oil.

Then came the **Nuclear Regulatory Commission (NRC)**, established in 1975. While framed as a consumer safety reform (splitting the old Atomic Energy Commission into a promoter and a regulator), the NRC's strict and slow licensing process effectively strangled the nuclear energy expansion that Project Independence depended on. Decades of permitting delays followed, nuclear development stalled, and American energy dependence on foreign sources *deepened* rather than shrinking.

The lesson: America announced a vision and then built the institutional machinery to prevent it from happening. This pattern — bold public goals paired with quiet structural sabotage — is one we've seen repeated ever since.

## PART I: ADDITIONAL CASE STUDIES
### *How America Has Regressed Since Reagan*

---

### Case Study 2: The Telecommunications Act of 1996 — Deregulation That Created Monopolies

This is perhaps the clearest example of regulatory language being used against its own stated purpose. The Telecommunications Act of 1996 was sold to the American public as a move to *increase competition* in phone, cable, and internet services. The promise: more providers, lower prices, better service.

What actually happened was the opposite. The Act's loose merger guidelines triggered a wave of consolidation. Hundreds of regional telecom companies were absorbed into a shrinking handful of giants. By the 2010s, most Americans had a choice of *one or two* internet providers — with no competitive pressure to improve speeds or lower prices. The U.S., which had *led* the world in communications infrastructure, fell to the middle of the pack in broadband speed and affordability compared to peer nations.

The lesson: deregulation without antitrust teeth doesn't create competition. It creates permission slips for monopoly. This is why the AWFixer Party separates *smart deregulation* from *naive deregulation* — the goal is a competitive market, not just a less-regulated one.

---

### Case Study 3: The Savings & Loan Crisis and the 2008 Financial Collapse — The Same Mistake, Twice

In the 1980s, Reagan-era deregulation loosened oversight of Savings & Loan institutions, allowing them to take on riskier investments while still being backed by federal deposit insurance. The result was predictable: reckless lending, massive failures, and a taxpayer bailout that cost roughly **$130 billion**.

America was handed a clear lesson. It didn't take it.

Twenty years later, the same structural dynamic — private profit, socialized risk — played out on a vastly larger scale. Financial deregulation throughout the 1990s and 2000s, including the repeal of Glass-Steagall in 1999, allowed commercial banks to behave like investment banks. When the housing market collapsed in 2008, the institutions that had spent years packaging and selling toxic mortgage debt came to the U.S. government with their hands out. The bailout this time was measured in the *trillions*.

In both cases, the political and financial establishments framed these as unforeseeable crises. They weren't. They were the logical outcome of removing guardrails and assuming that markets would self-correct. The pre-WWI Gilded Age had its own version of this — railroad and banking speculation, unchecked leverage, and a financial panic (1907) that finally forced the creation of the Federal Reserve. America keeps relearning this lesson because it keeps letting the people who benefit from forgetting it write the textbooks.

---

### Case Study 4: Healthcare Consolidation — Competition Promised, Oligopoly Delivered

In no sector is the gap between free-market rhetoric and free-market reality wider than in American healthcare. Since the 1990s, hospital systems, insurance companies, and pharmaceutical distributors have undergone massive consolidation — all under regulatory frameworks that nominally protect competition.

The result:
- **Hospital mergers** have repeatedly been shown to *raise prices* in the markets they affect, not lower them. Studies have found that hospital mergers in markets with little remaining competition have led to price increases of 20–40%.
- **Insurance consolidation** has left many regions of the country with one or two carriers offering employer plans, reducing the leverage workers have to demand better coverage.
- **Pharmaceutical pricing** operates in a system deliberately insulated from market pressure — the government is legally *prohibited* from negotiating Medicare drug prices in bulk, a carve-out written by the same legislators who receive pharmaceutical industry donations.

This is the pre-WWI Gilded Age model in modern dress: essential services controlled by a small number of powerful actors, with political influence sufficient to prevent the kind of competition that would actually serve the public. Standard Oil didn't call itself a monopoly either.

---

### Case Study 5: Infrastructure — A Century of Underinvestment

In 1919, a young Army officer named Dwight Eisenhower joined a military convoy crossing the United States by road. It took 62 days. The roads were so poor that vehicles broke down constantly and stretches of the route were essentially impassable. That experience stayed with him. Decades later, as President, he signed the Federal-Aid Highway Act of 1956, creating the Interstate Highway System — one of the most consequential public investments in American history.

The Eisenhower Interstate System was explicitly modeled on what he'd seen in Germany: the Autobahn. America looked at foreign infrastructure, recognized its own deficiency, and *did something about it*.

Today, the American Society of Civil Engineers gives U.S. infrastructure a C- grade. Bridges, water systems, rail, and roads are aging and underfunded. Meanwhile, countries like China, Japan, South Korea, and much of Western Europe have made sustained infrastructure investments that are measurable in productivity, quality of life, and economic competitiveness. America once built the Panama Canal, the Hoover Dam, and the Interstate System. It now struggles to repair the bridges it already has.

The regression here isn't just physical — it's philosophical. The postwar consensus that public investment in shared infrastructure was a national good has eroded. What replaced it was the assumption that private capital would fill the gap. Largely, it hasn't — because infrastructure that serves everyone equally is rarely as profitable as infrastructure that serves the wealthy selectively.

## PART II: THE POLICY PLATFORM
### *What AWFixer Actually Stands For*

---

### PILLAR ONE: Real Competition, Not Just Deregulation

**The Problem:**
Deregulation, as practiced over the past 40 years, has frequently been used as a tool to remove *consumer and worker protections* while leaving *corporate protections* intact. That's not a free market. That's a rigged one.

**AWFixer Policy Positions:**

- **Restore and strengthen antitrust enforcement.** The Sherman Antitrust Act was passed in 1890 for a reason. Market concentration in airlines, banking, agriculture, tech, healthcare, and telecom has reached levels that would have been recognizable to Teddy Roosevelt. We support aggressive use of existing antitrust law and modernizing it to address digital platform monopolies.

- **Deregulate barriers to entry, not consumer protections.** There's a meaningful difference between regulations that protect incumbents from competition (occupational licensing overreach, zoning laws that prevent housing construction, certificate-of-need laws that block new hospitals) and regulations that protect the public from harm (food safety, environmental standards, financial disclosures). We support eliminating the former while strengthening the latter.

- **End regulatory capture.** Regulatory agencies should not be staffed primarily by alumni of the industries they regulate. We support mandatory cooling-off periods, conflict-of-interest disclosures, and independent oversight of major regulatory bodies.

---

### PILLAR TWO: Pro-Business AND Pro-Revenue

**The Problem:**
The debate over taxes has been trapped in a false binary for decades: *low taxes* (which favor business and the wealthy) vs. *high taxes* (which fund public services but are framed as anti-growth). AWFixer rejects this framing entirely.

**AWFixer Policy Positions:**

- **Broaden the tax base rather than raise rates.** A healthy, competitive economy with fewer monopolies, more small and mid-size businesses, and a larger middle class generates *more tax revenue at lower marginal rates* than an economy dominated by a small number of mega-corporations using sophisticated strategies to reduce their effective tax rate to near zero. The goal isn't punishing success — it's making sure the system that enables success is funded by it.

- **Close loopholes before debating rates.** Before any conversation about raising or lowering tax rates, the AWFixer Party believes the existing structure should be enforced and simplified. The U.S. tax code is approximately 70,000 pages long, most of which exists to serve the interests of those who could afford to lobby for exceptions. A flatter, cleaner, loophole-free tax code would raise more revenue at lower headline rates and remove the competitive disadvantage that honest businesses face against those with expensive tax attorneys.

- **Tax investment in America's future, not income from labor.** The current system taxes wages more heavily than capital gains — meaning a nurse pays a higher effective rate than a hedge fund manager. AWFixer supports rebalancing this so that the rewards of economic growth are more broadly shared, and the people who physically build, serve, and maintain the country aren't disproportionately funding it.

- **Support small business over corporate welfare.** Corporate subsidies, targeted tax breaks, and preferential treatment for large companies at the expense of small competitors distort markets and concentrate power. We support eliminating industry-specific tax carve-outs and replacing them with broad, neutral incentives available to any business that invests in American workers or infrastructure.

---

### PILLAR THREE: Intellectual and Moral Honesty in Governance

**The Problem:**
American political culture rewards confidence over correctness, soundbites over substance, and tribal loyalty over honest reckoning. Politicians routinely advocate for policies they know to be ineffective because changing position is framed as weakness. The result is a governing class that optimizes for winning arguments rather than solving problems.

**AWFixer Policy Positions:**

- **Evidence-based policy, not ideology-based policy.** Every major policy proposal should include a clear statement of the problem being solved, the mechanism by which the proposed solution addresses it, and measurable outcomes that will be tracked over time. Policies that don't work should be revised or ended, regardless of which party proposed them.

- **Institutional term limits and rotation.** Career politicians develop incentives that diverge from those of their constituents over time. We support reasonable term limits for congressional leadership roles (not necessarily rank-and-file seats) and mandatory rotation off committee assignments to prevent the accumulation of entrenched power.

- **Mandatory conflict-of-interest disclosure.** Any elected official or senior appointee who votes on, regulates, or oversees an industry in which they hold financial interests should be required to divest or publicly disclose those interests before taking any relevant action.

- **Honest language in legislation.** Bills should be named for what they do, not for what sounds good. A bill that loosens environmental oversight should not be called the "Clean Future Act." We support a nonpartisan legislative plain-language review office empowered to flag misleading bill titles and summaries.

## PILLAR FOUR: Foreign Policy — Principled Engagement Over Perpetual Intervention

---

### The AWFixer Foreign Policy Doctrine

American foreign policy has, for the better part of three decades, operated on a default setting of intervention first, diplomacy second, and reflection rarely. The results speak for themselves: trillions of dollars spent, thousands of American lives lost, and a Middle East no more stable than when we arrived. Meanwhile, relationships with major global powers have calcified into adversarial postures that serve weapons contractors more than they serve the American people.

AWFixer believes in a foreign policy that is **strategically clear, diplomatically active, and militarily restrained** — one that protects Americans everywhere they are, builds durable partnerships, and treats engagement as the first tool rather than the last resort.

---

### PRINCIPLE ONE: Diplomacy Is Strength, Not Weakness

The framing of diplomacy as "soft" and military action as "strong" has cost America enormously. Diplomacy is the harder, longer, more intellectually demanding work — and historically, it produces more durable outcomes than force. The Marshall Plan rebuilt Western Europe and created 70 years of allied stability. The Abraham Accords demonstrated that patient relationship-building in the Middle East could yield normalization agreements that decades of conflict couldn't. Diplomacy isn't naive — it's the most powerful tool in a great nation's arsenal when wielded with patience and consistency.

**AWFixer Positions:**
- Restore and fully staff the State Department, which has experienced significant brain drain over the past decade
- Elevate diplomatic personnel to the same cultural status as military officers — they are both serving America
- Require that military options be formally preceded by documented diplomatic exhaustion before congressional authorization
- Invest in foreign language training, cultural expertise, and long-term regional specialists within the diplomatic corps — America has too often sent generalists into situations that required deep specialists

---

### PRINCIPLE TWO: The Middle East — Strategic Restraint With Clear Redlines

America's post-9/11 posture in the Middle East has produced two full-scale wars, numerous military interventions, and a generational commitment of resources and lives that has not meaningfully advanced American security or regional stability. AWFixer does not believe in abandonment — but we do believe in honest accounting.

**The AWFixer Middle East Position:**

We will not commit American military resources to Middle Eastern conflicts unless a threat to the United States is **direct, proportionate, and unmistakable.** Vague threats, proxy conflicts, and the internal political struggles of sovereign nations do not meet this threshold. America is not the world's police force, and the Middle East has demonstrated repeatedly that externally imposed solutions do not hold.

What we will do:
- **Maintain strong bilateral relationships** with regional partners, particularly Saudi Arabia and the UAE, as stabilizing economic and diplomatic anchors
- **Protect American citizens and assets** abroad with full force and without hesitation when they are directly threatened
- **Engage in counter-terrorism operations** that are targeted, intelligence-led, and time-limited — not open-ended occupations
- **Support diplomatic resolution** of conflicts like the Israeli-Palestinian situation through sustained multilateral engagement rather than episodic, crisis-driven summits that produce temporary headlines and no lasting framework

**What we will stop doing:**
- Committing to nation-building projects without defined exit conditions and measurable benchmarks
- Treating every regional power struggle as a binary choice between American intervention and catastrophe
- Allowing domestic political cycles to dictate foreign military commitments

---

### PRINCIPLE THREE: Strengthening Key Partnerships

**Saudi Arabia and the UAE**

The Kingdom of Saudi Arabia and the United Arab Emirates represent two of the most consequential relationships America has in the world — not merely for energy, but for regional influence, economic partnership, and the stabilizing role both nations increasingly play in an otherwise volatile region. The Abraham Accords showed what coordinated Gulf-state diplomacy can accomplish. AWFixer believes in deepening these relationships with long-term strategic frameworks, not transactional deal-making that shifts with each administration.

This means:
- Consistent, predictable American partnership that Gulf leaders can rely on across political transitions
- Expanding economic and technology cooperation beyond the defense sector
- Honest dialogue on human rights concerns through private diplomatic channels rather than public shaming that damages the relationship without producing change
- Recognizing the UAE in particular as a model of rapid modernization that America should be engaging as a peer partner in economic development and regional diplomacy

**Japan**

Japan remains one of America's most important and underappreciated strategic relationships. A democratic, technologically advanced, economically powerful nation in the Indo-Pacific, Japan is a natural anchor for any American strategy in the region. AWFixer supports:

- Deepening the U.S.-Japan security alliance with genuine reciprocity — not an arrangement where America provides security while Japan provides bases
- Expanding collaboration on semiconductor manufacturing, advanced technology, and supply chain resilience — areas where American and Japanese interests are nearly perfectly aligned
- Supporting Japan's evolving defense posture and treating it as the peer partner it is, not a protectorate

---

### PRINCIPLE FOUR: Reintegrating Russia and China — Engagement Over Alienation

This is perhaps the most consequential foreign policy position AWFixer takes, and we take it deliberately.

**The current approach isn't working.**

Twenty years of treating Russia as a permanent adversary to be isolated has produced a Russia more aligned with China, more aggressive on its borders, and more isolated from the international norms that Western engagement was supposed to reinforce. The expansion of NATO eastward, whatever its strategic logic, was experienced by Russia as encirclement and has been used domestically to justify exactly the kind of authoritarian consolidation the West claimed to oppose. We are not excusing Russian behavior — we are observing that the response to it has consistently made it worse.

Similarly, the U.S.-China relationship has devolved from a complex interdependence into an increasingly adversarial posture that carries genuine risks of miscalculation. Decoupling the world's two largest economies entirely is neither achievable nor desirable — it raises the cost of goods for American consumers, disrupts global supply chains, and removes the economic incentives that have historically been the most effective moderating force on Chinese behavior.

**AWFixer does not believe in naive engagement.** We are not suggesting America ignore territorial aggression, human rights violations, or cyberattacks. What we are saying is that:

- **Sustained diplomatic engagement** with both Russia and China, at the highest levels and across multiple domains, produces better outcomes than strategic isolation
- **Economic interdependence**, carefully structured to protect American interests and security, is a more durable moderating force than sanctions regimes that have failed to change behavior in either country
- **Multilateral frameworks** — arms control, climate, trade, health security — require Russian and Chinese participation to function. Excluding them from these conversations doesn't protect America; it removes the leverage those conversations provide
- A foreign policy that treats 2.5 billion people and two nuclear-armed great powers as permanent enemies is not a strategy — it's an indefinitely deferred crisis

The path forward with both nations is long, difficult, and uncertain. AWFixer is under no illusions about that. But the current path leads somewhere worse, and someone has to be willing to say so.

---

### PRINCIPLE FIVE: The North Korea Initiative — A Rare Window

North Korea represents one of the most isolated and heavily militarized states on earth, a nuclear-armed nation of 26 million people cut off from the global community by ideology, fear, and the self-interest of an entrenched ruling class. Every American administration since the Korean War has essentially treated North Korea as a problem to be managed rather than a situation to be resolved.

AWFixer believes the anticipated leadership transition in North Korea presents a **rare and time-limited opportunity** that history may not offer again.

**The Logic:**

Leadership transitions in deeply insular authoritarian states are moments of genuine uncertainty. A new leader — whether by succession, reform from within, or structural change — faces a different set of incentives than an entrenched one. They may be more open to the international legitimacy, economic development, and security guarantees that engagement could offer, particularly if the inherited system is visibly failing to meet the material needs of the population.

South Korea's economic miracle is visible to North Koreans in ways that were impossible twenty years ago. The gap in living standards between the two Koreas is the single most powerful argument for integration, and it is increasingly impossible to hide.

**The AWFixer North Korea Program — Proposed Framework:**

We propose the development of a formal, multi-phase engagement program with the following structure:

**Phase 1 — Back-Channel Contact and Trust Building (Years 1–2)**
- Establish back-channel diplomatic contact through neutral intermediaries — Switzerland, Sweden, and Singapore have all played this role historically
- Offer a formal, public statement of non-aggression and non-regime-change intent as a good-faith opening position
- Begin low-level humanitarian cooperation on areas of mutual interest: food security, disease prevention, and natural disaster response
- Engage China as a co-sponsor of the engagement framework, giving China a constructive role and aligning its incentives with stability rather than dependency

**Phase 2 — Economic Integration Pathway (Years 3–7)**
- Develop a formal economic development offer: a Marshall Plan-style framework that provides infrastructure investment, agricultural development, and industrial capacity in exchange for verifiable steps toward denuclearization and opening
- Establish special economic zones modeled on China's 1980s reform experience — a proven pathway for authoritarian states to open economically without requiring immediate political transformation
- Invite North Korean technical and scientific personnel to international institutions under structured exchange programs
- Normalize limited travel and cultural exchange

**Phase 3 — Security Framework and Normalization (Years 7–15)**
- Pursue a formal peace treaty to replace the 1953 armistice — the Korean War has technically never ended, and this unresolved legal status is itself a barrier to normalization
- Work toward a Korean Peninsula nuclear-free zone through multilateral security guarantees
- Support the gradual reintegration of North Korea into international bodies — trade organizations, scientific institutions, cultural and athletic exchange
- Model the long-term vision on the German reunification experience while acknowledging the significant differences — reunification may or may not be the destination, but integration into the global community is

**Why This Could Work:**

The argument against engaging North Korea has always been that the regime will take what it's offered without meaningful reciprocity. That argument has merit — but it describes the current situation too. North Korea has nuclear weapons, ballistic missile technology, and no meaningful incentive to change course. Continued isolation has not produced denuclearization. It has produced a more capable nuclear program and a population with no stake in international norms.

A new leader, facing the challenge of governing a failing economy and a population with increasing awareness of the outside world, may calculate differently than their predecessor. AWFixer believes America should be ready to meet that moment with a credible, generous, and carefully structured offer — one that makes integration more attractive than isolation.

This is not appeasement. It is strategy.

# AWFixer Party — Labor & Immigration Policy

---

## PILLAR FIVE: Labor & Immigration — Fix the Incentives, Not the People

---

### The AWFixer Core Insight

Most American political parties treat labor and immigration as separate issues requiring separate solutions. AWFixer recognizes them as **the same problem wearing different hats.**

The immigration debate in America has been almost entirely focused on the supply side — how many people are coming, how to stop them, how to find and remove those already here. This is the wrong frame entirely. It is expensive, ineffective, and inhumane. It also deliberately avoids the more uncomfortable question:

**Why are they being hired?**

Because someone is hiring them. Consistently. At scale. For decades. And that someone is making a business decision that American labor policy has made not only possible, but *economically rational.*

AWFixer believes that if you remove the economic incentive to hire foreign labor over American labor, you solve the immigration problem without militarizing the border, without mass deportation operations, and without tearing apart communities. You also solve significant chunks of the labor problem at the same time — because the same forces that suppress wages through outsourcing and undocumented labor also suppress wages through offshoring, automation incentives, and union erosion.

Fix the incentive structure. The behavior follows.

---

### PRINCIPLE ONE: Remove the Incentive to Hire Foreign Labor

**The Problem:**

American businesses hire undocumented workers and offshore American jobs for the same fundamental reason — it's cheaper. Not because American workers are unwilling to do the work, but because the system has been structured, deliberately and over time, to make exploitable labor more accessible and less accountable than fairly compensated domestic labor.

The enforcement model — raids, deportations, border walls — attacks the symptom while leaving the disease entirely intact. The day after a workplace raid, the economic incentive to hire cheap, unprotected labor still exists. Someone will fill that gap.

**AWFixer Policy Positions:**

- **Employer accountability over worker punishment.** Shift the enforcement focus dramatically toward the businesses and individuals doing the hiring. Meaningful, consistently enforced penalties for hiring undocumented workers — not token fines that are priced in as a cost of doing business, but penalties scaled to the size and profitability of the employer — change the calculation at the source.

- **Close the outsourcing subsidy.** American tax law currently allows corporations to defer taxes on profits earned abroad and to deduct the costs of moving operations overseas. This means American taxpayers are effectively subsidizing the export of American jobs. AWFixer supports eliminating these deductions and closing offshore profit-sheltering mechanisms so that outsourcing reflects its true cost rather than an artificially subsidized one.

- **Level the playing field on labor costs.** When an American company outsources manufacturing to a country with no environmental standards, no worker safety requirements, and wages held artificially low by authoritarian suppression, they aren't competing — they're arbitraging a human rights deficit. AWFixer supports import standards that require goods entering the American market to meet baseline labor and environmental benchmarks, creating a floor that genuine competition can operate above.

- **Simplify and accelerate legal work authorization.** Part of what drives undocumented labor is that the legal pathway is slow, expensive, and dysfunctional. Employers who genuinely need specialized foreign labor should have a fast, transparent, and accountable process for obtaining it — one that prevents abuse rather than incentivizing workarounds. A functioning legal system reduces the pressure that drives the illegal one.

- **Pathways, not purges.** For the estimated 10–12 million undocumented people already living and working in the United States, AWFixer does not support mass deportation. It is logistically impossible, fiscally ruinous, and morally untenable. We support a structured pathway to legal status — not amnesty without conditions, but a defined process involving back taxes, registration, background checks, and demonstrated community ties. People who have built lives here are not the problem. The system that made their situation economically inevitable is.

### PRINCIPLE TWO: Reforming Labor Unions Through Independent Oversight

This is a position that will make some enemies. AWFixer accepts that.

**The Honest Assessment:**

Labor unions were one of the most important institutions in the development of the American middle class. The 40-hour work week, the weekend, workplace safety standards, employer-provided healthcare, the end of child labor in industry — these did not come from the goodwill of corporations. They came from organized labor, often at significant personal cost to the workers who fought for them.

That history deserves respect. What it does not deserve is a free pass on the present.

American labor unions today are a mixed picture. Some remain effective advocates for their members. Many have become something closer to political fundraising machines with a labor branding problem. Decades of lobbying Congress — the same Congress that regulates, funds, and oversees labor law — has produced a cozy interdependency that serves union leadership and political donors while delivering diminishing returns for rank-and-file workers.

Union membership in the private sector has declined from roughly 35% of the workforce in the 1950s to under 6% today. This is not entirely the fault of unions — aggressive corporate union-busting, legislative erosion of organizing rights, and the structural shift away from manufacturing have all played a role. But the unions themselves have not been blameless. Corruption, leadership entrenchment, and the prioritization of political influence over member outcomes have damaged credibility and weakened organizing efforts at exactly the moments when workers needed them most.

It is intellectually dishonest to look at this trajectory and declare the current structure sound.

**AWFixer Policy Positions:**

- **Establish an Independent Labor Union Oversight Board (ILUOB).** This body would be structured similarly to an independent judiciary — appointed through a nonpartisan process, insulated from congressional lobbying pressure, with fixed terms and conflict-of-interest requirements. Its mandate would be to:
    - Audit union finances and ensure member dues are being spent in members' interests
    - Investigate and prosecute union corruption without political interference
    - Ensure democratic governance within unions — secret ballots, transparent leadership elections, member access to financial records
    - Mediate disputes between union leadership and membership when the two are demonstrably misaligned

- **Separate union political activity from union labor activity.** Unions should be extraordinarily effective at negotiating wages, benefits, working conditions, and job security. Political donations and lobbying activity should be strictly separated from dues-funded operations and require explicit, individual member opt-in. A worker who wants union representation should not be required to fund political positions they may not share.

- **Restore and modernize organizing rights.** The decline of private sector union membership has not been a neutral market outcome. It has been the result of decades of legislative and legal erosion of the right to organize. AWFixer supports restoring meaningful organizing protections — including stronger penalties for illegal retaliation against organizing workers and streamlined election processes — while pairing those restored rights with the accountability structures described above. Stronger unions that are genuinely accountable to their members are good for workers, and ultimately good for the economy.

- **Extend oversight to public sector unions as well.** The conflict of interest in public sector union negotiations — where unions bargain against elected officials whose campaigns they helped fund — is a structural problem that neither party has been willing to address honestly. AWFixer supports the same oversight framework applying to public sector unions, with specific attention to the pension and benefit commitments that have created long-term fiscal crises in numerous states and municipalities.

### PRINCIPLE THREE: Civil Standard of Living Over Minimum Wage

This is one of the most genuinely original positions in the AWFixer platform, and it deserves careful explanation.

**The Problem With Minimum Wage:**

The minimum wage debate is one of the most reliably unproductive arguments in American politics. One side picks a number and calls it justice. The other side picks the same number and calls it a job-killer. Neither side is entirely wrong, and neither side is solving the actual problem.

The minimum wage is a blunt instrument applied to a complex system. A dollar figure set in Washington means something very different in rural Mississippi than it does in San Francisco. It also measures only one dimension — hourly cash wages — while ignoring the full picture of what it actually costs to live a dignified human life in America today.

**The AWFixer Alternative: The Civil Standard of Living (CSL)**

Instead of mandating a minimum hourly wage, AWFixer proposes the development and maintenance of a **Civil Standard of Living Index** — a regularly updated, regionally adjusted measure of what it actually costs to meet a baseline of dignified existence in America.

The CSL would account for:
- Housing (rent or mortgage at non-exploitative market rates for the region)
- Nutritious food
- Healthcare, including preventive care and mental health
- Transportation (including non-car options where relevant)
- Childcare and dependent care
- Utilities including broadband internet, which is now an employment prerequisite
- A modest discretionary component — because a life with no capacity for leisure, culture, or savings is not a dignified one

**Why This Works Better:**

Rather than mandating a dollar figure, the CSL creates a **publicly visible benchmark** against which compensation packages — wages plus benefits plus non-cash compensation — can be measured. The policy mechanisms AWFixer would attach to this benchmark are designed to make meeting it the path of least resistance for businesses, rather than the legally required floor:

- **Tax incentives for CSL-compliant employers.** Businesses that demonstrably compensate their full-time workforce at or above the regional CSL — counting wages, healthcare contributions, retirement matching, and other material benefits — receive favorable tax treatment. The government isn't dictating pay; it's making fair pay the smarter financial decision.

- **Public procurement preferences.** Federal and state contracting would favor CSL-compliant employers. If you want government business, you take care of the people doing the work.

- **Transparency requirements.** Large employers would be required to publish their median total compensation relative to the regional CSL, creating public accountability and reputational incentives without mandating specific dollar figures.

- **Regional calibration.** Because the CSL is indexed to actual cost of living by region, it avoids the bluntness of a single national number. A small business in rural Alabama and a tech firm in Seattle are operating in genuinely different economic environments. The CSL acknowledges that reality instead of pretending it doesn't exist.

- **Regular updates.** The CSL would be recalculated annually by an independent economic body — not a politically appointed committee with an agenda, but career economists using standardized methodology. This removes the political theater of minimum wage fights from the legislative calendar.

**The Philosophical Difference:**

Minimum wage sets a floor and dares businesses to stay above it. The Civil Standard of Living defines what a human life in America actually requires, makes that information publicly available and economically relevant, and creates conditions where meeting it is the rational business choice. # AWFixer Party — Energy Policy

---

## PILLAR SIX: Energy Policy — Nuclear Deregulation and the Honest Environmental Argument

---

### The AWFixer Energy Doctrine

America's energy debate has been trapped for decades in a false choice between economic growth and environmental responsibility, between fossil fuels and unreliable renewables, between energy independence and environmental conscience. AWFixer rejects this framing entirely.

There is an energy source that is:
- **Carbon-free** in operation
- **Reliable** regardless of weather, season, or time of day
- **Scalable** to meet industrial, residential, and transportation demand simultaneously
- **Land-efficient** to a degree no other clean energy source approaches
- **Proven** over seven decades of operation across dozens of countries

That source is nuclear power. And America has been systematically dismantling its capacity to use it for fifty years.

This is not an accident. It is the result of regulatory capture, institutional inertia, fossil fuel industry influence, and a public fear campaign built on misunderstood science. AWFixer intends to name all of that clearly and reverse course.

---

### The Historical Failure — Revisiting Project Independence

As noted in our founding case studies, this story begins with Nixon's Project Independence in 1973 and the Nuclear Regulatory Commission that quietly strangled it. That history is worth dwelling on before we talk about solutions, because the mechanism of failure then is the same mechanism we must dismantle now.

The NRC was created in 1975 with a legitimate purpose — separating the promotional and regulatory functions of nuclear oversight that had been uncomfortably combined in the old Atomic Energy Commission. No serious person objects to independent safety regulation of nuclear facilities. That is reasonable and necessary.

What happened next was not reasonable or necessary.

The NRC's licensing process became one of the most expensive, time-consuming, and unpredictable regulatory gauntlets in the entire American economy. A nuclear plant that might take five years to build in France or South Korea takes fifteen to twenty years to license and build in the United States — if it gets built at all. Licensing applications run to millions of pages. Designs certified as safe can be challenged mid-construction on grounds that were available at the start. Cost overruns driven by regulatory delay become arguments against nuclear's economic viability — conveniently ignoring that the regulatory process itself created the cost overruns.

Meanwhile, the fossil fuel industry spent lavishly to ensure that nuclear's primary competitor for baseload power — natural gas — faced no comparable regulatory burden. The result was entirely predictable: American nuclear capacity stagnated, gas expanded, and the country that pioneered commercial nuclear power now builds essentially none of it while France generates 70% of its electricity from nuclear and pays some of the lowest electricity prices in Europe.

We built the cage. We locked ourselves in it. AWFixer intends to open the door.

---

### The Honest Environmental Argument

AWFixer is willing to say something that most pro-environment politicians are not: **not all clean energy is created equal, and pretending otherwise is intellectually dishonest.**

The dominant political narrative treats solar, wind, and hydroelectric power as unambiguous environmental goods and nuclear as a dangerous legacy technology to be phased out. The evidence does not support this narrative.

**Solar Fields: The Hidden Footprint**

Utility-scale solar generation requires enormous amounts of land. To generate the same amount of electricity that a single nuclear plant produces, a solar installation requires roughly **75 times the land area.** That land, typically in desert or semi-arid ecosystems, is not empty. It supports plant communities, reptile populations, migratory bird corridors, and soil ecosystems that took centuries to develop. Large-scale solar development in the American Southwest has already come into direct conflict with tortoise habitats, native plant communities, and the open landscapes that define those environments.

Solar panels also require mining. Significant quantities of silicon, silver, indium, tellurium, and rare earth elements go into panel manufacturing — mining operations that carry their own environmental costs, largely externalized to the countries where the extraction occurs. Panels have a lifespan of 25–30 years, after which they become a rapidly growing waste stream that current recycling infrastructure is not equipped to handle at scale.

None of this means solar is bad. Rooftop solar on existing structures is genuinely low-impact and AWFixer supports its expansion aggressively. What we are saying is that utility-scale solar fields are not the environmental free lunch they are often presented as. They are a significant land use choice with real ecological consequences that deserve honest accounting.

**Hydroelectric Dams: Proven Ecological Destruction**

The environmental case against large-scale hydroelectric power is, if anything, stronger and more thoroughly documented than the case against solar fields — yet it receives far less political attention because hydro has been grandfathered into the "clean energy" category without scrutiny.

Large dams do the following, consistently and measurably:

- **Destroy river ecosystems.** The reservoir behind a dam floods the river valley, eliminating the riparian habitat that supports the greatest concentration of biodiversity of any terrestrial environment. The Snake River dams in the Pacific Northwest have driven multiple salmon runs to the edge of extinction. The Three Gorges Dam in China displaced over a million people and eliminated the habitat of the Yangtze river dolphin, which is now functionally extinct.

- **Fragment fish migration.** Even with fish ladders, dams are barriers to the migratory patterns that anadromous fish — salmon, steelhead, shad, sturgeon — have evolved over millions of years. Fish ladders help. They do not solve the problem.

- **Alter downstream hydrology permanently.** Dams trap sediment that would otherwise nourish river deltas and coastal wetlands. The Colorado River, one of the most dammed in the world, no longer reaches the sea. The delta that once supported one of the most productive estuaries in North America is now a dry salt flat.

- **Release methane.** Reservoirs in tropical and subtropical regions produce significant methane emissions from decomposing organic matter — a greenhouse gas far more potent than CO₂ over short timeframes. Large hydroelectric projects in some climates produce more greenhouse gas per kilowatt-hour than natural gas plants.

The environmental movement has largely given hydroelectric a pass because it predates modern ecological awareness and because it is renewable in a narrow technical sense. AWFixer believes this inconsistency undermines the credibility of energy policy debates. If we are serious about environmental impact, we must be consistent about measuring it.

**Nuclear: The Environmental Case For**

Against this honest accounting of solar and hydro, nuclear's environmental profile looks remarkably strong:

- **Land use:** A nuclear plant producing 1,000 megawatts occupies roughly one square mile including all facilities and exclusion zones. The equivalent solar installation would require 75 square miles. The equivalent wind installation would require over 300 square miles.

- **Carbon emissions:** Nuclear power's lifecycle carbon emissions — including mining, construction, operation, and decommissioning — are comparable to wind and lower than solar on a per-kilowatt-hour basis. They are roughly 70 times lower than natural gas and 100 times lower than coal.

- **Waste:** Nuclear waste is real, it is dangerous, and it must be managed responsibly. It is also remarkably small in volume. All of the spent nuclear fuel ever produced by American commercial reactors would fit within an area the size of a football field stacked roughly ten yards deep. It is solid, it does not disperse, and it can be isolated. Compare this to the waste stream of fossil fuel combustion, which is released directly into the atmosphere in quantities measured in billions of tons annually. The nuclear waste problem is serious and solvable. The fossil fuel waste problem is civilization-scale and currently unaddressed.

- **Wildlife:** Nuclear plants occupy small footprints and their exclusion zones — ironically — often become de facto wildlife refuges. The exclusion zone around Chernobyl, despite the accident, has become one of the most wildlife-rich areas in Europe simply because humans have not been present for forty years. Nuclear power, operated correctly, is one of the least ecologically disruptive large-scale energy sources that exists.

---

### The Safety Argument — Confronting Fear With Facts

No serious energy policy conversation about nuclear can avoid the safety question. AWFixer addresses it directly.

**The statistical reality is this:** nuclear power has the lowest deaths-per-unit-of-energy-produced of any power source in the world, including wind and solar. This is not a pro-nuclear talking point. It is the consistent finding of peer-reviewed energy safety research across multiple independent institutions.

Coal kills roughly 25 people per terawatt-hour of electricity produced, primarily through air pollution. Natural gas kills about 3. Wind kills about 0.04 — mostly from installation and maintenance accidents. Nuclear kills about **0.03**, a figure that *includes* the deaths attributable to Chernobyl and Fukushima.

Chernobyl was a catastrophic accident involving a reactor design that Western nations never used, operated by a government that actively suppressed safety concerns and delayed evacuation. It killed fewer people than coal kills every week. Fukushima, caused by a 9.0 magnitude earthquake and the largest tsunami in recorded Japanese history, killed one person from radiation. One. The evacuations that followed — which AWFixer acknowledges were driven by understandable public fear — killed hundreds through the stress and disruption of displacement.

The public fear of nuclear power is not irrational — it is a predictable response to vivid, dramatic events amplified by decades of cultural messaging from films, books, and advocacy. But policy should not be driven by availability bias. It should be driven by evidence. And the evidence is unambiguous: nuclear power is extraordinarily safe, and treating it as uniquely dangerous while burning millions of tons of coal and gas annually is a policy choice that kills people.

### The AWFixer Nuclear Deregulation Program

**1. Overhaul the NRC Licensing Process**

The current NRC licensing timeline is incompatible with building new nuclear capacity at the speed climate goals require. AWFixer supports:

- Establishing a **hard timeline** for licensing decisions — applications must receive a final determination within three years of submission, with clear criteria for approval, denial, or required modification
- Moving to **design certification rather than site-by-site review** for approved reactor designs. If a reactor design has been certified as safe, individual plants using that design should require substantially less review than a novel first-of-kind installation
- **Streamlining the appeal and intervention process** to prevent regulatory delay from being weaponized by opponents who have exhausted legitimate technical objections but continue to litigate process
- Maintaining rigorous safety standards throughout — deregulation here means removing bureaucratic obstruction, not removing safety requirements

**2. Embrace Advanced and Small Modular Reactor Technology**

The nuclear industry of 2026 is not the nuclear industry of 1975. A new generation of reactor designs addresses many of the concerns — cost, safety, waste, scale — that have been used to argue against nuclear expansion:

- **Small Modular Reactors (SMRs)** can be factory-manufactured and deployed at a fraction of the cost and construction time of traditional large-scale plants. They can be scaled to match community energy needs, sited on smaller footprints, and deployed in regions where a full-scale nuclear plant would be impractical
- **Generation IV reactor designs** — including molten salt, high-temperature gas, and fast neutron reactors — offer passive safety systems that physically cannot produce a Chernobyl-style runaway reaction. Some designs can consume existing spent fuel waste as part of their fuel cycle, addressing the waste problem and the fuel supply simultaneously
- **Fusion research** deserves sustained federal investment. Practical fusion power remains a long-term goal but recent advances in private fusion development suggest the timeline may be shorter than conventional wisdom assumed. America should be leading this race, not watching it

**3. Phase Out Oil and Gas on a Defined Timeline**

AWFixer believes in honesty. An honest energy transition acknowledges several things simultaneously:

- Oil and gas cannot be eliminated overnight without catastrophic economic disruption — the infrastructure, supply chains, and industrial processes that depend on fossil fuels cannot be replaced faster than alternatives can be built
- A defined, credible phase-out timeline is essential — without it, the fossil fuel industry will continue to make long-term investments that lock in decades of additional carbon emissions, and the political will to transition will continue to dissipate
- The fastest path away from fossil fuels is through nuclear baseload power, which can replace gas-fired electricity generation at scale and reliability that no current renewable combination can match
- Workers in fossil fuel industries deserve transition support — retraining programs, economic development investment in affected communities, and honest acknowledgment that this transition will require adjustment

**AWFixer proposed timeline:**
- **Years 1–5:** Expedite NRC reform; fast-track SMR deployment approvals; begin phase-out of coal-fired generation with nuclear replacement priority
- **Years 5–15:** Scale SMR deployment nationally; begin systematic replacement of gas-fired baseload with nuclear; expand rooftop solar and storage as distributed complements
- **Years 15–30:** Complete coal and oil phase-out from electricity generation; accelerate industrial and transportation electrification from a nuclear-dominated grid; reassess natural gas role in hard-to-electrify industrial processes

**4. Invest in a National Nuclear Workforce**

America's nuclear engineering and skilled trades pipeline has atrophied alongside the industry itself. Rebuilding it requires:

- Federal investment in nuclear engineering programs at universities and technical colleges
- Apprenticeship and certification programs for the construction trades that build and maintain nuclear facilities
- Competitive compensation frameworks that make nuclear careers attractive relative to the oil and gas sector, which has historically offered strong wages

---

### Why This Is the Environmentally Serious Position

AWFixer anticipates that this platform will draw criticism from environmental advocates who have built their identity around opposition to nuclear power. We respect that advocacy tradition while disagreeing with its conclusions on this specific point.

The measure of an environmental policy is not how it feels or what it signals — it is whether it results in less damage to the natural systems that human and non-human life depend on. On that measure, the continued opposition to nuclear power while simultaneously opposing fossil fuels has contributed to an impossible policy gridlock that has benefited one actor above all others: the fossil fuel industry.

Every year that passes without new nuclear capacity is a year filled with natural gas. Every nuclear plant that closes is replaced, in practice, not by wind and solar but by gas peaker plants that run whenever the sun isn't shining and the wind isn't blowing. Germany's celebrated *Energiewende* — the aggressive shutdown of nuclear in favor of renewables — resulted in Germany burning more coal and paying higher electricity prices while its carbon emissions *increased* in the short term.

The planet does not care about ideological consistency. It cares about how many tons of carbon are being released and how many acres of habitat are being destroyed. On both measures, nuclear is the most defensible large-scale energy choice available to us right now, with the technology we actually have, at the scale the problem actually requires.

That is AWFixer's position. We are prepared to defend it.

# AWFixer Party — Fiscal Transparency Policy

---

## PILLAR SEVEN: The Public Ledger — Radical Transparency in Federal Spending

---

### The AWFixer Core Argument

The federal government spent approximately $6.5 trillion in fiscal year 2023. The average American citizen has essentially no meaningful visibility into where that money went, who authorized each expenditure, what it was supposed to accomplish, or whether it did. The official budget documents that do exist run to thousands of pages of deliberate complexity, published in formats and language designed — whether intentionally or through institutional inertia — to resist comprehension by the people whose money it represents.

This is not acceptable in 2026. The technology to make every dollar of public spending visible, traceable, and permanently recorded exists right now. We use it to track cryptocurrency transactions worth fractions of a cent across a global network in real time. There is no technical reason the United States federal budget cannot operate on the same principle of radical, immutable transparency.

The only reason it doesn't is that opacity is useful to the people who benefit from it.

AWFixer intends to end that.

---

### The Model — What Blockchain-Style Transparency Actually Means

The core innovation of cryptocurrency ledger technology is not the currency itself — it is the **distributed, immutable, publicly verifiable record** that underlies it. Every transaction is recorded permanently, timestamped, traceable to its origin, and impossible to alter retroactively without the alteration itself being visible.

Applied to federal spending, this means:

- Every appropriation, expenditure, contract award, grant, transfer, and payment made with public funds is recorded as a permanent, timestamped entry
- Every entry is linked to the authorizing legislation, the authorizing official, the recipient, and the stated purpose
- The ledger is **public by default** — accessible to any citizen, journalist, researcher, or oversight body without a Freedom of Information request, without a waiting period, and without redaction except in narrowly defined and independently reviewed national security categories
- The ledger is **immutable** — entries cannot be deleted or altered. Corrections are recorded as new entries that reference and supersede the original, so the correction history is itself visible
- The ledger is **distributed** — maintained across multiple independent nodes including federal agencies, independent oversight bodies, academic institutions, and potentially state governments, so no single actor can manipulate or suppress it

This is not a radical or untested technology concept. It is the straightforward application of principles that have been proven at global scale to the management of public funds.

---

### What Gets Recorded

**Every dollar. No exceptions outside a narrow, independently reviewed national security carve-out.**

Specifically:

**Federal Budget Appropriations**
- Every line item passed by Congress, linked to the bill text and the voting record of every member who authorized it
- Conference committee changes visible alongside original House and Senate versions
- Continuing resolutions and emergency appropriations recorded with the same granularity as regular budget items

**Agency and Departmental Spending**
- Every contract award above a de minimis threshold — AWFixer proposes $1,000 — including the vendor, the amount, the stated purpose, the contracting officer, and the competitive bidding record
- Every grant awarded, including the applicant pool, the selection criteria, and the reviewing officials
- Every inter-agency transfer
- Every expenditure on travel, conferences, consulting, and administrative overhead
- Payroll aggregates by department and grade level — not individual salaries, which carry privacy considerations, but band-level distributions that allow meaningful scrutiny of staffing costs

**Congressional Spending**
- Member office budgets and expenditures
- Committee spending
- Earmarks — restored to visibility after the brief period of earmark transparency reform and subsequent erosion

**Institutional Spending**
- Any institution receiving federal funds — universities, hospitals, think tanks, contractors, nonprofits — would be required to report the disposition of those funds on the public ledger with the same granularity required of federal agencies
- This closes the current loophole where federal money disappears into institutional budgets with minimal downstream accountability

**Lobbying and Political Expenditure Connections**
- While the ledger itself records spending rather than political activity, AWFixer proposes that the ledger system be linked to existing lobbying disclosure databases so that citizens can trace, in a single interface, the connection between a contract award and any lobbying activity by the recipient in the relevant period

---

### The National Security Carve-Out — And Why It Must Be Narrow

AWFixer acknowledges that some federal spending genuinely cannot be disclosed publicly without compromising national security — covert operations, intelligence sources and methods, certain weapons development programs. We are not naive about this.

However, the current national security classification system has become a vast, largely self-administered exemption from accountability that bears almost no relationship to genuine security requirements. The Pentagon has failed its audit — every single year — for decades. The last time the Department of Defense passed a comprehensive audit was never. It has never passed one. The classified budget, which runs to tens of billions of dollars annually, receives essentially no independent scrutiny.

AWFixer's approach to the national security carve-out:

- **Classified expenditures are recorded on the ledger in aggregate form** — the total spent by each classified program, the authorizing legislation, and the oversight committee responsible, without the operational details that would compromise security
- **An independent Inspector General with full ledger access** reviews classified expenditures and certifies annually that the classification is genuinely necessary — not merely convenient
- **Automatic declassification timelines** apply to spending records, with a default of ten years unless specifically renewed through a documented and visible process
- **The existence of a classified program cannot itself be classified** — a lesson learned from decades of black budget abuse. Americans have a right to know that money is being spent on something, even if they cannot know on what

---

### Implementation — The Federal Public Ledger Authority

AWFixer proposes the creation of a **Federal Public Ledger Authority (FPLA)** — an independent agency modeled structurally on the Federal Reserve, insulated from direct congressional and executive interference, with the following mandate:

**Design and maintain the ledger infrastructure**
- Built on open-source distributed ledger technology, auditable by independent technologists
- Designed for public accessibility — a searchable, visualizable interface that does not require technical expertise to use meaningfully
- API access for journalists, researchers, and civic organizations to build their own analytical tools on top of the raw data

**Set and enforce reporting standards**
- Uniform data standards across all federal agencies so that spending data is comparable and aggregable
- Compliance monitoring with escalating consequences for agencies that fail to report accurately or on time
- Technical assistance for smaller agencies and institutions that lack reporting infrastructure

**Operate the independent oversight function**
- The FPLA's Inspector General division would have subpoena power, whistleblower protection authority, and the ability to refer findings to the Department of Justice
- Annual public reports on spending patterns, anomalies, and compliance failures
- Real-time anomaly flagging — the ledger system would include automated pattern recognition to identify expenditures that deviate from baseline norms and flag them for human review

**Protect privacy while maximizing transparency**
- Individual employee salaries, personal beneficiary information, and certain contractor personnel data would be aggregated or anonymized
- The privacy protection standard would be publicly defined and narrow — transparency is the default, privacy protection is the exception that requires justification

---

### Why This Changes Everything

The argument for fiscal transparency is usually framed as an anti-corruption measure. That is true and important. But AWFixer believes the benefits go substantially further:

**It changes the incentive structure for spending decisions.** When every expenditure is permanently and publicly visible, the calculation for an official considering a questionable contract award or a wasteful program changes fundamentally. The question is no longer "will anyone find out" — because the answer is automatically yes. The question becomes only "can I defend this."

**It enables genuine democratic accountability.** Congressional oversight of federal spending is currently dependent on bureaucratic reporting processes that agencies control, hearings that occur years after the relevant spending, and the limited resources of inspector general offices. A public ledger means that 330 million Americans, thousands of journalists, and hundreds of research institutions are simultaneously scrutinizing federal spending in real time. No oversight committee, however diligent, can match that.

**It creates a feedback loop between taxation and expenditure.** Americans pay taxes with essentially no visibility into what those taxes buy. This disconnection is not accidental — it is what allows the gap between what politicians promise and what spending actually delivers to persist indefinitely. When the connection between a tax dollar and its destination is visible and traceable, the political conversation about both taxation and spending changes.

**It forces honest accounting of program effectiveness.** When spending is linked to stated purposes and those records are permanent, the question of whether a program delivered what it cost becomes answerable by anyone. Failed programs can no longer be quietly renewed. Successful ones can be identified and scaled. The evidence base for policy improves when the spending data is genuinely accessible.

**It restores the premise of public trust.** The foundational claim of democratic governance is that the government's authority derives from the consent of the governed and that public money is held in trust for the public. A government that spends $6.5 trillion a year in effective opacity is not honoring that premise. A public ledger does not fix every problem with American governance — but it restores the basic condition that makes fixing those problems possible: the ability to see what is actually happening.

---

### Addressing the Objections

**"This would be a massive technical undertaking."**
Yes. It would also be less complex than the systems currently operated by major financial institutions, which process millions of transactions per second with full audit trails. The federal government's annual transactions, while numerous, are well within the capacity of proven distributed ledger technology. The cost of building this system is trivial compared to the waste it would expose.

**"Agencies would game the reporting."**
Some would try. This is why the FPLA has enforcement authority and why the ledger's immutability means that manipulated entries leave traces. Gaming a transparent system is substantially harder than gaming an opaque one — the manipulation itself becomes visible.

**"This would harm national security."**
Only if national security is defined to include protecting wasteful and unaccountable spending from public scrutiny. The narrow genuine security carve-out described above is sufficient to protect operational security. The Pentagon's inability to pass an audit is not a national security issue. It is an accountability failure being laundered as one.

**"Private contractors would resist disclosure of their federal contracts."**
Correct. Their resistance is not a valid objection. Federal contracts are public money. The terms on which public money changes hands are public information. Contractors who find this unacceptable are not obligated to seek federal business.

## PILLAR EIGHT: Criminal Justice — Accountability, Reform, and the Value of Useful Work

---

### The AWFixer Core Argument

American criminal justice policy has spent decades oscillating between two positions that both manage to be wrong simultaneously. One side demands increasingly punitive sentencing, mass incarceration, and a system that warehouses human beings at extraordinary public expense while producing recidivism rates that demonstrate, empirically, that warehousing does not work. The other side responds with decarceration, reduced sentencing, and a rhetorical softness on consequences that fails victims, undermines public safety, and is politically untenable to the majority of Americans who correctly observe that a society without meaningful consequences for harmful behavior is not a functioning society.

AWFixer's position is simpler and more honest than either:

**You cannot have nice things and be soft on crime.** A functioning civilization — with the infrastructure, institutions, and public trust that make quality of life possible — requires that people who damage it face real consequences. That is not cruelty. That is the basic social contract.

**But consequences and rehabilitation are not opposites.** The smartest consequence for most criminal behavior is not a cage. It is structured, demanding, meaningful work — work that builds something, teaches something, and earns something. Work that connects punishment to contribution rather than to mere suffering.

These two positions are not in tension. They are, in fact, the same position stated from different angles.

### The Honest Assessment of American Incarceration

The United States incarcerates more people per capita than any other nation on earth — approximately 2 million people at any given time, at a cost of roughly $40,000 per inmate per year, totaling over $80 billion annually in direct costs before accounting for the downstream economic losses of removing working-age adults from their families and communities.

The recidivism rate — the proportion of released prisoners who are rearrested within five years — is approximately 77%.

Read that number again. **Seventy-seven percent.** Three out of four people released from American prisons are rearrested within five years. By any honest metric, the current system is not working. It is not rehabilitating. It is not deterring. It is cycling the same population through an enormously expensive institutional revolving door while achieving essentially nothing except temporarily removing people from society at great public cost.

AWFixer believes this is unacceptable — not because the consequences are too harsh, but because they are **consequential without being constructive.** Suffering without purpose does not build better citizens. It builds people who are institutionalized, unemployable, disconnected from civil society, and statistically likely to reoffend.

The answer is not to remove consequences. It is to make consequences serve a purpose beyond punishment.

### The AWFixer Criminal Justice Framework

---

### PRINCIPLE ONE: Consequences Are Non-Negotiable

Before discussing reform, AWFixer states clearly what it believes about crime and consequences:

- **Victims matter first.** The criminal justice system exists primarily to protect citizens and provide justice to those harmed. Reform that loses sight of this is not reform — it is abdication. Every policy position in this pillar is evaluated against its effect on public safety and victim justice before its effect on offender outcomes.

- **Consequences must be real and certain.** Deterrence research consistently shows that the *certainty* of consequences matters more than their *severity*. A criminal justice system that prosecutes selectively, plea-bargains reflexively, and releases people quickly teaches that consequences are negotiable. AWFixer supports fully resourced prosecution, reduced reliance on plea bargaining for serious offenses, and sentencing structures that are clear, consistent, and actually served.

- **Violent and predatory offenders are not the target of reform programs.** The AWFixer reform model described below is designed for non-violent offenders, property criminals, and people whose offenses are rooted in addiction, poverty, or circumstance rather than predatory intent. Violent offenders, repeat predators, and those who pose genuine ongoing threats to public safety will be incarcerated for the duration of their sentences. This distinction is not soft — it is precise.

- **Order is a prerequisite for everything else.** Safe streets, functioning businesses, thriving communities — none of these exist without baseline public order. AWFixer supports fully funded, professionally trained, and rigorously accountable law enforcement as a non-negotiable foundation of everything else in this platform.

### PRINCIPLE TWO: The AWFixer Reform-Work Program

Here is where AWFixer departs from both the punitive right and the permissive left with a proposal that takes both rehabilitation and accountability seriously.

**The Core Concept:**

America needs things built. It has a nuclear construction program to execute, infrastructure to repair, desert land that needs development for energy and water projects, and a federal estate that requires constant maintenance. It also has a large population of non-violent offenders sitting in cells at $40,000 a year, learning nothing, contributing nothing, and leaving no better equipped to function in society than when they arrived.

AWFixer proposes a **National Reform-Work Corps** — a structured program in which eligible non-violent offenders serve their sentences not in traditional correctional facilities but in supervised construction and development camps, primarily in remote and desert locations, where they contribute to meaningful national infrastructure projects while receiving vocational training, education, and structured preparation for reintegration.

This is not a novel idea. Work programs have existed in American corrections for well over a century. What AWFixer proposes is different in three important ways: the **scale** is national and tied to real infrastructure needs, the **skills training** is genuine and industry-certified rather than performative, and the **compensation structure** creates a stake in the outcome for the participant.

### PRINCIPLE THREE: Program Design

**Who Qualifies:**

Eligibility for the Reform-Work Corps requires:
- Non-violent offense classification
- No history of predatory or sexually violent behavior
- Minimum sentence of one year remaining
- Voluntary participation — the program is opt-in, not compulsory, though the incentives to participate are substantial
- Psychological assessment confirming suitability for the program structure

**Where:**

Remote and desert locations serve multiple purposes simultaneously:

- **Security through geography.** The isolation of desert construction sites eliminates the need for the expensive physical infrastructure of traditional prisons while providing natural deterrence to escape. A person who walks off a construction site in the Mojave Desert faces a more effective deterrent than most prison walls — and one that costs nothing to build.

- **Alignment with national needs.** The Southwest deserts are precisely where nuclear plants, solar infrastructure, water reclamation facilities, and transmission line corridors need to be built. The Reform-Work Corps sites are not arbitrary — they are located where America's infrastructure agenda requires human labor.

- **Genuine separation from society during active sentences.** AWFixer does not apologize for this. A sentence is a sentence. The remoteness of the work sites means participants are genuinely serving their time, genuinely separated from the society they harmed, and genuinely unable to reoffend during that period. This satisfies the accountability requirement without the soul-destroying idleness of traditional incarceration.

**What They Build:**

Directly connected to the AWFixer infrastructure and energy agenda:

- Nuclear plant construction and site preparation — the single largest skilled labor need in the AWFixer energy program
- Desert water reclamation and pipeline infrastructure
- Transmission line corridors for the new energy grid
- Federal highway and bridge repair in remote corridors
- Public land conservation and restoration projects
- Federal building construction and renovation

The output is real. It has dollar value. It reduces the cost of infrastructure projects that would otherwise be paid entirely to private contractors. This matters for the program's political sustainability — it has to produce something visible and valuable to maintain public support.

**What They Learn:**

Every Reform-Work Corps participant is enrolled in a structured vocational curriculum tied to the work being performed:

- **Construction trades:** Electrical, plumbing, concrete, welding, heavy equipment operation — all taught to industry certification standards and formally credentialed through partnerships with trade unions and certification bodies
- **Nuclear plant construction trades:** A specific pipeline into the skilled labor shortage that the AWFixer nuclear expansion program will create — one of the few areas where demand for certified workers will dramatically outpace supply in the coming decade
- **Basic and applied education:** Literacy, numeracy, and GED completion for those who need it; technical education tied to construction trades for those who don't
- **Financial literacy and reintegration preparation:** Because one of the primary drivers of recidivism is the inability to function economically after release — no bank account, no credit history, no understanding of how to manage money

**Compensation:**

AWFixer believes in earned stakes. Reform-Work Corps participants receive:

- A **daily wage** below market rate but meaningful — sufficient to accumulate savings over the course of a sentence
- A **reintegration fund** — a portion of wages held in escrow and released upon successful completion of the sentence, providing a financial foundation for the transition back to society that currently does not exist for most released prisoners
- **Reduced sentence consideration** tied to performance, certification completion, and behavior — not automatic, not guaranteed, but available through a transparent and consistently applied process
- **Formal trade certifications** that are portable, industry-recognized, and do not carry a convict designation — a certified welder is a certified welder

**What This Costs Versus What It Produces:**

Traditional incarceration: $40,000 per inmate per year, zero productive output, 77% recidivism.

Reform-Work Corps: Higher upfront cost per participant due to supervision and training infrastructure, partially offset by the productive output of construction labor. The net cost per participant is higher in the short term. The net cost per *successful reintegration* — meaning a person who leaves the program, enters the workforce, pays taxes, and does not return to the criminal justice system — is dramatically lower when the 77% recidivism rate of traditional incarceration is factored into the comparison.

AWFixer does not pretend this program solves every problem. Some participants will fail. Some will reoffend. The program will need rigorous evaluation, honest reporting, and willingness to modify what doesn't work. But the baseline it is competing against — a system with a 77% failure rate at $40,000 per year — is not a high bar.

### PRINCIPLE FOUR: The Front End — Prevention and Accountability

Reform-Work Corps addresses what happens after conviction. AWFixer also has positions on what happens before:

**Drug Policy:**

The War on Drugs has been, by measurable outcomes, one of the most expensive failed policies in American history. It has not reduced drug use. It has not eliminated drug markets. It has produced mass incarceration of primarily non-violent offenders, devastated communities, enriched criminal organizations, and cost hundreds of billions of dollars. AWFixer supports:

- **Decriminalization of personal use quantities** for substances where the primary harm is to the user — consistent with the reform-first philosophy that incarceration is not the appropriate response to self-destructive behavior
- **Aggressive prosecution of distribution and trafficking** — the supply side of drug markets causes genuine harm to communities and deserves genuine consequences
- **Treatment as the first-line response** to addiction — including mandatory treatment as an alternative to incarceration for first and second offense possession charges
- **Honest accounting of what the War on Drugs has cost and produced** — without this, the political will to change it cannot be built

**Policing:**

AWFixer supports law enforcement fully and without apology. We also believe that full support for law enforcement means demanding that it be professional, trained, accountable, and effective — not insulating it from scrutiny when it fails to meet those standards.

- **Full funding** for police departments, including competitive compensation that attracts and retains capable officers
- **Investment in training** — particularly in de-escalation, mental health crisis response, and the growing share of police calls that are not criminal matters but social crises being handled by the only 24-hour responder available
- **Genuine accountability** for misconduct — not performative, not union-shielded, not dependent on the same department investigating itself. The independent oversight model AWFixer applies to labor unions applies here too
- **Mental health co-responder programs** — a significant proportion of police use-of-force incidents involve people in mental health crisis. Sending a mental health professional alongside or instead of an officer in appropriate circumstances is not soft — it is the right tool for the actual problem

**Reentry:**

The back end of the criminal justice system — the moment of release — is where American policy fails most catastrophically and most expensively. A person released from prison with $50, no ID, no housing, no job prospects, and a criminal record that disqualifies them from most employment is a person statistically destined to return. AWFixer supports:

- **Automatic expungement** of non-violent records after a defined period of successful reintegration — a criminal record that follows someone forever is not justice, it is a permanent subsidy to recidivism
- **Ban the box** legislation preventing employers from asking about criminal history on initial applications — with exceptions for positions involving vulnerable populations or fiduciary responsibility
- **Reentry housing programs** — the first 90 days after release are the highest-risk period for reoffending; structured transitional housing with support services during this window has among the best cost-benefit ratios of any criminal justice intervention
- **Voting rights restoration** upon completion of sentence — a person who has served their time has paid their debt and should be fully reintegrated into civil society, including the franchise

### PRINCIPLE FIVE: The Philosophical Consistency

AWFixer acknowledges that this platform will be attacked from both sides. The right will call the reform elements soft. The left will call the work program exploitative.

To the right: recidivism is the ultimate measure of a criminal justice system. A 77% failure rate is not tough — it is expensive and ineffective. Demanding that consequences produce better citizens rather than merely warehouse them is not softness. It is the highest possible standard for what punishment should accomplish.

To the left: consequences matter. Victims matter. Public safety is not a luxury position available only to people who haven't been harmed by crime. A reform program that takes work seriously, that builds real skills, that produces real infrastructure, and that pays participants for their labor is not exploitation — it is the most dignified and constructive use of time that a sentence can involve. The alternative is a cell.

AWFixer is not interested in feeling good about criminal justice policy. We are interested in producing a society where fewer people become criminals, fewer victims are created, fewer people cycle through an expensive and ineffective system, and the ones who do emerge from it better equipped to contribute than when they went in.

That is tough on crime. That is also smart on crime. In AWFixer's view, there is no meaningful difference between the two.

# AWFixer Party — Anti-Corruption Policy

---

## PILLAR NINE: Government Corruption — A Comprehensive Reckoning Across Every Branch, Every Level, and Every Uniform

---

### The AWFixer Core Argument

American anti-corruption discourse has a compartmentalization problem. When corruption is discussed in the context of the judiciary, it stays there. When it surfaces in Congress, the conversation is confined to campaign finance and ethics committees that are, almost without exception, staffed by the very people they are supposed to oversee. When it emerges in the executive branch, it becomes a partisan weapon rather than a systemic diagnosis. When it appears in the military, it is quietly absorbed by an institutional culture that has historically treated internal accountability as a threat to unit cohesion and public confidence.

The result is a government in which corruption is addressed nowhere comprehensively because every institution has successfully argued that its corruption is somebody else's problem, or a unique circumstance requiring special handling, or a matter of national security, or simply not as bad as it looks.

AWFixer rejects this entirely.

Corruption is not a judicial problem or a legislative problem or an executive problem or a military problem. It is a **power problem.** Wherever power concentrates without accountability, corruption follows. This is not a controversial observation — it is one of the oldest and most thoroughly documented findings in the entire history of political thought. The American system was designed with this explicitly in mind. The separation of powers, the Bill of Rights, the impeachment mechanism — these are all anti-corruption architecture.

That architecture has been allowed to decay. AWFixer proposes to rebuild it — comprehensively, without institutional carve-outs, and without the pretense that any branch or level of government is too important, too sensitive, or too trusted to require scrutiny.

---

### The Scale of the Problem — An Honest Inventory

Before proposing solutions, AWFixer insists on naming the problem clearly. The following is not a partisan attack on any administration or party. It is a structural inventory of where corruption has taken root across American governance.

**The Legislative Branch**

Congress has constructed, over decades, a set of ethical standards for itself that would be considered inadequate in most professional fields and illegal in many private sector contexts.

Members of Congress are permitted to trade individual stocks while simultaneously sitting on committees that oversee the industries in which they are trading. The STOCK Act of 2012 was supposed to address this. Its enforcement mechanism — a $200 fine for late disclosure — has proved so toothless that violations are routine and consequence-free. Multiple studies have shown that members of Congress consistently outperform market averages at rates that are statistically difficult to explain without access to non-public information.

The revolving door between congressional staff, members, and the lobbying industry is not merely a conflict of interest — it is the primary mechanism through which legislation is written to serve industry rather than the public. Staff members spend years developing expertise and relationships at public expense, then monetize that expertise and those relationships in the private sector at salaries that make their government compensation look like an internship stipend. The knowledge and access that public service produces are not public property under the current system. They are personal assets to be sold.

Congressional ethics committees are, structurally, a bad joke. Members investigate members. Committees are staffed with an eye toward protecting institutional reputation rather than enforcing standards. The House Ethics Committee has a decades-long history of investigations that begin with fanfare and conclude with nothing. The Senate Ethics Committee is, if anything, less active. The incentive to investigate a colleague is always weaker than the incentive to extend the same professional courtesy you hope to receive when your own conduct is questioned.

**The Executive Branch**

Executive branch corruption operates at two levels that rarely receive equal attention.

The first is the visible, dramatic level — the cabinet official enriching a former employer through a favorable regulatory decision, the political appointee awarding contracts to donors, the department head using public resources for personal benefit. These cases occasionally produce headlines and sometimes produce consequences, though rarely consequences proportionate to the offense.

The second is the structural level, which is both more pervasive and more damaging. The executive branch's regulatory agencies — the FDA, the EPA, the SEC, the FCC, the CFTC, and dozens of others — are systematically staffed with personnel whose professional careers have been, and will again be, tied to the industries they regulate. This is not incidental. It is the predictable result of the fact that the most qualified people to regulate an industry are the people who have worked in it — and those people bring with them loyalties, assumptions, and future employment calculations that inevitably shape regulatory outcomes.

This is regulatory capture, and it is arguably the most consequential form of corruption in the American system because it operates continuously, invisibly, and entirely within the law.

**The Judicial Branch**

Federal judges, including Supreme Court justices, are subject to remarkably limited financial disclosure requirements and essentially no binding ethics code at the Supreme Court level. This situation — which would be considered inadequate for a county clerk in most states — persisted for decades without serious reform, sustained by the institutional mythology that federal judges are simply too distinguished to require the oversight applied to lesser officials.

Recent years have produced extensive documentation of undisclosed gifts, travel, and hospitality received by sitting Supreme Court justices from parties with interests before the Court. The dollar values involved are significant. The failure to disclose is documented. The consequences have been essentially nonexistent.

Below the Supreme Court, federal district and circuit judges face disclosure requirements but enforcement is weak, recusal standards are largely self-administered, and the process by which cases are assigned — and by which certain judges become reliably favorable venues for certain types of litigation — has received insufficient scrutiny.

At the state level, judicial elections in many states have become vehicles for direct investment by interests seeking favorable courts, with campaign contributions flowing from parties who subsequently appear before the judges they helped elect. The Supreme Court's ruling in *Caperton v. Massey* established that extreme cases of this dynamic violate due process, but the threshold is high and the practice continues broadly.

**State and Local Government**

Corruption at the state and local level is arguably more directly harmful to ordinary Americans than federal corruption because it governs the interactions most people actually have with government — building permits, contracts, zoning decisions, law enforcement, school administration, and public works.

The mechanisms are consistent: contracting corruption, where public contracts are awarded through processes that are nominally competitive but practically predetermined; zoning and permitting corruption, where development approvals follow political relationships rather than planning criteria; law enforcement corruption, where departments operate with minimal oversight and internal accountability cultures that treat misconduct as a personnel matter rather than a public safety crisis; and electoral corruption, where the combination of weak campaign finance enforcement and concentrated local political power creates conditions for systematic manipulation.

The federal government's capacity to address state and local corruption is limited by constitutional structure and by the practical reality that federal prosecution resources are finite. AWFixer's approach addresses this at the structural level rather than relying entirely on federal enforcement.

**The Military — A Special and Serious Reckoning**

The military occupies a unique position in American culture — one that has, for understandable historical reasons, been treated as largely exempt from the public scrutiny applied to civilian institutions. AWFixer respects the service and sacrifice of military personnel at every level. That respect is precisely why we believe the military deserves better accountability than it currently receives.

The documented problems are serious and have been insufficiently addressed:

*Financial accountability* is perhaps the starkest failure. As noted in the fiscal transparency pillar, the Department of Defense has never passed a comprehensive audit. Ever. The DoD's budget — currently over $850 billion annually — is the largest discretionary expenditure in the federal government, and it has operated for decades without the basic financial controls that any private company of comparable size would be legally required to maintain. This is not a technical accounting problem. It is a structural accountability failure of the first order.

*Procurement corruption* in the defense industry represents one of the most durable and expensive forms of corruption in American government. The revolving door between Pentagon leadership and defense contractors is institutionalized to a degree that makes the civilian regulatory revolving door look modest. Generals and admirals retire and immediately assume board positions and senior advisory roles at the companies whose contracts they helped award. Cost overruns of hundreds of percent on major weapons systems are routine and consequence-free. The F-35 program — the most expensive weapons acquisition in human history — has cost over $400 billion and counting, has missed performance specifications consistently, and has produced accountability for no one at any level.

*Sexual assault and harassment* within the military represents a documented, systemic failure that has persisted despite decades of official acknowledgment and promised reform. The Military Justice Improvement Act and its successors have attempted to address the fundamental structural problem — that sexual assault cases are investigated and prosecuted within the same chain of command that the accused typically serves in — but implementation has been slow, resistance from within the institutional culture has been substantial, and survivor outcomes remain deeply inadequate. AWFixer treats this not as a cultural problem to be gradually improved but as a justice failure requiring structural remedy.

*Hazing, abuse, and toxic command culture* have produced documented patterns of harm to service members that are inadequately investigated and rarely result in meaningful accountability. The deaths of soldiers like Vanessa Guillén — whose 2020 murder at Fort Hood exposed a pattern of harassment complaints that were systematically ignored — represent the visible tip of a much larger problem of internal complaint suppression within military commands.

*Contractor abuse overseas* — including documented cases involving private military contractors operating under DoD contracts — has created accountability vacuums where serious misconduct, up to and including homicide, has occurred in environments where neither military justice nor civilian law applies effectively.

AWFixer does not raise these issues to undermine the military or diminish the genuine service of the overwhelming majority of personnel who serve honorably. We raise them because a military that cannot hold itself accountable is not only failing its own members — it is accumulating the kind of institutional rot that, historically, has preceded far more serious failures.

### The AWFixer Anti-Corruption Architecture

---

### INSTITUTION ONE: The Independent Anti-Corruption Commission (IACC)

The centerpiece of AWFixer's anti-corruption policy is the creation of a genuinely independent federal anti-corruption body — not an inspector general embedded within the agencies it oversees, not an ethics committee staffed by the institution's own members, but a structurally independent commission with jurisdiction across all three branches of the federal government and investigative authority over state and local corruption involving federal funds.

**Structure:**

The IACC is modeled on independent anti-corruption bodies that have demonstrated effectiveness in comparable democracies — notably the Independent Commission Against Corruption in Hong Kong, which in two decades transformed one of the world's most corrupt governments into one of its cleaner ones, and similar bodies in Australia and Singapore.

- **Seven commissioners** appointed through a process deliberately designed to prevent single-party or single-branch control: two appointed by the President, two by congressional leadership of each party, two by a panel of federal appellate judges selected by lot, and one by a civic selection panel drawn from nonpartisan civil society organizations
- **Fixed eight-year terms**, non-renewable, staggered to prevent simultaneous replacement
- **Removal only by supermajority** of the full commission plus independent judicial review — not removable by the appointing authority
- **Dedicated budget** constitutionally protected from legislative reduction below a defined floor — preventing the defunding that has historically been used to neutralize oversight bodies

**Jurisdiction:**

- All three branches of the federal government including, explicitly, the Supreme Court and the military
- State and local officials in matters involving federal funds or federal constitutional violations
- Private contractors, lobbyists, and other non-governmental actors engaged in corrupt relationships with government officials
- Former officials for conduct during their government service, with no statute of limitations for conduct that was actively concealed

**Powers:**

- Subpoena authority over documents, financial records, and testimony
- Independent prosecution authority — the IACC can bring cases without routing through the Department of Justice, eliminating the conflict of interest inherent in asking the executive branch to prosecute itself
- Asset freezing authority pending investigation of financial corruption
- Whistleblower protection and management — the IACC is the designated safe harbor for government whistleblowers, with the authority to investigate retaliation independently of the agency involved
- Public reporting — the IACC publishes detailed public reports on all closed investigations, including those that did not result in prosecution, with findings of fact that inform public accountability even when criminal prosecution is not warranted

### INSTITUTION TWO: Comprehensive Ethics Reform by Branch

**Congress:**

- **Mandatory stock divestiture.** Members of Congress, upon taking office, divest individual stock holdings into blind trusts or index funds. This is not a radical proposal — it is the standard applied to executive branch cabinet officials. There is no coherent argument for why it should not apply equally to legislators.
- **Lifetime lobbying ban** for members; five-year ban for senior staff. The revolving door is not a perk of public service. The relationships and knowledge developed at public expense are public assets.
- **Real STOCK Act enforcement.** The $200 fine for disclosure violations is replaced with penalties scaled to the value of the undisclosed trades, mandatory disgorgement of profits from trades made during periods of relevant committee service, and criminal referral for patterns of violation.
- **Independent ethics enforcement.** The House and Senate ethics committees are replaced with an independent Congressional Ethics Authority staffed by retired federal judges, former prosecutors, and civic appointees — none of whom are current or former members of Congress.
- **Earmark transparency.** Every earmark, budget rider, and spending directive attached to legislation must be publicly attributed to the sponsoring member on the public ledger described in Pillar Seven, at the time of insertion — not disclosed months later in appendices that no one reads.

**The Executive Branch:**

- **Mandatory cooling-off periods** of five years before any executive branch official or senior career employee can work in an industry they regulated, advised on, or contracted with during their government service
- **Contractor disclosure** — all contracts awarded to firms that have employed former officials of the awarding agency within the previous ten years must be disclosed with that relationship explicitly flagged on the public ledger
- **Political appointee financial disclosure** at the same level currently required of judges — full asset disclosure, spousal income disclosure, and ongoing annual updates
- **Regulatory capture audit** — the IACC conducts a biennial audit of the personnel composition of major regulatory agencies, tracking the proportion of leadership drawn from regulated industries and publishing findings publicly

**The Judicial Branch:**

- **Binding Supreme Court ethics code.** The Supreme Court is not exempt from the ethics standards applied to lower federal courts. AWFixer supports legislation establishing a binding code of conduct for Supreme Court justices with enforcement through the IACC rather than through self-administration
- **Mandatory recusal standards** with independent review — a justice or judge who declines to recuse despite a disclosed relationship with a party must provide a written public explanation, which is subject to IACC review
- **Full financial disclosure** for all federal judges including gifts, travel, hospitality, and speaking fees, with the same disclosure standards applied to executive branch officials
- **Judicial appointment transparency** — the process by which federal judges are recommended, vetted, and nominated should be documented and publicly visible, with explicit disclosure of any communication between nominees and senators or White House officials outside of formal hearings

### INSTITUTION THREE: Military Accountability Reform

AWFixer treats military accountability as a separate and serious category requiring dedicated structural remedies rather than the continued promise of cultural change that has characterized thirty years of inadequate reform.

**Financial Accountability:**

- **Mandatory DoD audit completion within four years**, with the IACC empowered to investigate impediments to audit completion as potential obstruction
- **Criminal referral** for financial officers who certify accounts they know to be inaccurate — the same standard applied to corporate executives under Sarbanes-Oxley applied to defense financial management
- **Procurement reform** with independent cost estimation — major weapons programs require cost and performance estimates from bodies independent of the program office and independent of the contractors bidding on the work, with IACC review of programs that exceed estimates by more than 15%
- **Revolving door restrictions** identical to those applied to civilian agencies — retired generals and admirals are subject to a five-year cooling-off period before employment with defense contractors whose contracts they influenced
