## This is a draft of our philosophy

**Welcome to the AWFixer Party**

The AWFixer Political Party is a new political movement built on honest governance, economic reform, and a willingness to say what others won't. Our core values aren't talking points — they're commitments.

---

**Core Values**

**1. Deregulation and Competition Drive Down Prices**
Monopolies and regulatory capture don't protect consumers — they protect incumbents. When industries are shielded from competition by red tape, prices rise and quality falls. We believe that smart deregulation, paired with strong antitrust enforcement, forces businesses to compete for customers rather than lobby for protection. The result is lower prices, more innovation, and a marketplace that actually works for ordinary Americans.

**2. Pro-Business Means More Tax Revenue**
Supporting business doesn't mean cutting taxes into oblivion — it means creating the conditions where businesses *thrive and pay their share*. A healthy, growing economy with a broad tax base generates far more public revenue than a system riddled with loopholes built for the well-connected. We are pro-business *and* pro-accountability. Those aren't opposites.

**3. Intellectual and Moral Honesty in Leadership**
Too many political parties govern by poll numbers. We believe leadership requires the courage to be right even when it's unpopular — and the humility to admit when you're wrong. We hold ourselves to a higher standard of reasoning and ethical consistency, and we expect our representatives to do the same.

---

**How America Lost Its Way: 30 Years of Regression**

Since Ronald Reagan left office in 1989, the United States has quietly drifted back toward the conditions that defined the pre-WWI Gilded Age — concentrated wealth, weakened labor, and government that serves industry over citizens. The following examples illustrate this pattern.

---

**Case Study 1: The Nixon Administration and the Betrayal of Energy Independence**

Though predating Reagan, the Nixon era offers a foundational example of America's two-faced approach to governance — promising one thing publicly while doing another structurally.

In 1973, in response to the OPEC oil embargo, Nixon launched **Project Independence** — an ambitious national goal to achieve full U.S. energy self-sufficiency by 1980. The rhetoric was bold: America would never again be held hostage to foreign oil.

Then came the **Nuclear Regulatory Commission (NRC)**, established in 1975. While framed as a consumer safety reform (splitting the old Atomic Energy Commission into a promoter and a regulator), the NRC's strict and slow licensing process effectively strangled the nuclear energy expansion that Project Independence depended on. Decades of permitting delays followed, nuclear development stalled, and American energy dependence on foreign sources *deepened* rather than shrinking.

The lesson: America announced a vision and then built the institutional machinery to prevent it from happening. This pattern — bold public goals paired with quiet structural sabotage — is one we've seen repeated ever since.

## PART I: ADDITIONAL CASE STUDIES
### *How America Has Regressed Since Reagan*

### Case Study 2: The Telecommunications Act of 1996 — Deregulation That Created Monopolies

This is perhaps the clearest example of regulatory language being used against its own stated purpose. The Telecommunications Act of 1996 was sold to the American public as a move to *increase competition* in phone, cable, and internet services. The promise: more providers, lower prices, better service.

What actually happened was the opposite. The Act's loose merger guidelines triggered a wave of consolidation. Hundreds of regional telecom companies were absorbed into a shrinking handful of giants. By the 2010s, most Americans had a choice of *one or two* internet providers — with no competitive pressure to improve speeds or lower prices. The U.S., which had *led* the world in communications infrastructure, fell to the middle of the pack in broadband speed and affordability compared to peer nations.

The lesson: deregulation without antitrust teeth doesn't create competition. It creates permission slips for monopoly. This is why the AWFixer Party separates *smart deregulation* from *naive deregulation* — the goal is a competitive market, not just a less-regulated one.

---

### Case Study 3: The Savings & Loan Crisis and the 2008 Financial Collapse — The Same Mistake, Twice

In the 1980s, Reagan-era deregulation loosened oversight of Savings & Loan institutions, allowing them to take on riskier investments while still being backed by federal deposit insurance. The result was predictable: reckless lending, massive failures, and a taxpayer bailout that cost roughly **$130 billion**.

America was handed a clear lesson. It didn't take it.

Twenty years later, the same structural dynamic — private profit, socialized risk — played out on a vastly larger scale. Financial deregulation throughout the 1990s and 2000s, including the repeal of Glass-Steagall in 1999, allowed commercial banks to behave like investment banks. When the housing market collapsed in 2008, the institutions that had spent years packaging and selling toxic mortgage debt came to the U.S. government with their hands out. The bailout this time was measured in the *trillions*.

In both cases, the political and financial establishments framed these as unforeseeable crises. They weren't. They were the logical outcome of removing guardrails and assuming that markets would self-correct. The pre-WWI Gilded Age had its own version of this — railroad and banking speculation, unchecked leverage, and a financial panic (1907) that finally forced the creation of the Federal Reserve. America keeps relearning this lesson because it keeps letting the people who benefit from forgetting it write the textbooks.

### Case Study 4: Healthcare Consolidation — Competition Promised, Oligopoly Delivered

In no sector is the gap between free-market rhetoric and free-market reality wider than in American healthcare. Since the 1990s, hospital systems, insurance companies, and pharmaceutical distributors have undergone massive consolidation — all under regulatory frameworks that nominally protect competition.

The result:
- **Hospital mergers** have repeatedly been shown to *raise prices* in the markets they affect, not lower them. Studies have found that hospital mergers in markets with little remaining competition have led to price increases of 20–40%.
- **Insurance consolidation** has left many regions of the country with one or two carriers offering employer plans, reducing the leverage workers have to demand better coverage.
- **Pharmaceutical pricing** operates in a system deliberately insulated from market pressure — the government is legally *prohibited* from negotiating Medicare drug prices in bulk, a carve-out written by the same legislators who receive pharmaceutical industry donations.

This is the pre-WWI Gilded Age model in modern dress: essential services controlled by a small number of powerful actors, with political influence sufficient to prevent the kind of competition that would actually serve the public. Standard Oil didn't call itself a monopoly either.

### Case Study 5: Infrastructure — A Century of Underinvestment

In 1919, a young Army officer named Dwight Eisenhower joined a military convoy crossing the United States by road. It took 62 days. The roads were so poor that vehicles broke down constantly and stretches of the route were essentially impassable. That experience stayed with him. Decades later, as President, he signed the Federal-Aid Highway Act of 1956, creating the Interstate Highway System — one of the most consequential public investments in American history.

The Eisenhower Interstate System was explicitly modeled on what he'd seen in Germany: the Autobahn. America looked at foreign infrastructure, recognized its own deficiency, and *did something about it*.

Today, the American Society of Civil Engineers gives U.S. infrastructure a C- grade. Bridges, water systems, rail, and roads are aging and underfunded. Meanwhile, countries like China, Japan, South Korea, and much of Western Europe have made sustained infrastructure investments that are measurable in productivity, quality of life, and economic competitiveness. America once built the Panama Canal, the Hoover Dam, and the Interstate System. It now struggles to repair the bridges it already has.

The regression here isn't just physical — it's philosophical. The postwar consensus that public investment in shared infrastructure was a national good has eroded. What replaced it was the assumption that private capital would fill the gap. Largely, it hasn't — because infrastructure that serves everyone equally is rarely as profitable as infrastructure that serves the wealthy selectively.

## PART II: THE POLICY PLATFORM
### *What AWFixer Actually Stands For*

---

### PILLAR ONE: Real Competition, Not Just Deregulation

**The Problem:**
Deregulation, as practiced over the past 40 years, has frequently been used as a tool to remove *consumer and worker protections* while leaving *corporate protections* intact. That's not a free market. That's a rigged one.

**AWFixer Policy Positions:**

- **Restore and strengthen antitrust enforcement.** The Sherman Antitrust Act was passed in 1890 for a reason. Market concentration in airlines, banking, agriculture, tech, healthcare, and telecom has reached levels that would have been recognizable to Teddy Roosevelt. We support aggressive use of existing antitrust law and modernizing it to address digital platform monopolies.

- **Deregulate barriers to entry, not consumer protections.** There's a meaningful difference between regulations that protect incumbents from competition (occupational licensing overreach, zoning laws that prevent housing construction, certificate-of-need laws that block new hospitals) and regulations that protect the public from harm (food safety, environmental standards, financial disclosures). We support eliminating the former while strengthening the latter.

- **End regulatory capture.** Regulatory agencies should not be staffed primarily by alumni of the industries they regulate. We support mandatory cooling-off periods, conflict-of-interest disclosures, and independent oversight of major regulatory bodies.

---

### PILLAR TWO: Pro-Business AND Pro-Revenue

**The Problem:**
The debate over taxes has been trapped in a false binary for decades: *low taxes* (which favor business and the wealthy) vs. *high taxes* (which fund public services but are framed as anti-growth). AWFixer rejects this framing entirely.

**AWFixer Policy Positions:**

- **Broaden the tax base rather than raise rates.** A healthy, competitive economy with fewer monopolies, more small and mid-size businesses, and a larger middle class generates *more tax revenue at lower marginal rates* than an economy dominated by a small number of mega-corporations using sophisticated strategies to reduce their effective tax rate to near zero. The goal isn't punishing success — it's making sure the system that enables success is funded by it.

- **Close loopholes before debating rates.** Before any conversation about raising or lowering tax rates, the AWFixer Party believes the existing structure should be enforced and simplified. The U.S. tax code is approximately 70,000 pages long, most of which exists to serve the interests of those who could afford to lobby for exceptions. A flatter, cleaner, loophole-free tax code would raise more revenue at lower headline rates and remove the competitive disadvantage that honest businesses face against those with expensive tax attorneys.

- **Tax investment in America's future, not income from labor.** The current system taxes wages more heavily than capital gains — meaning a nurse pays a higher effective rate than a hedge fund manager. AWFixer supports rebalancing this so that the rewards of economic growth are more broadly shared, and the people who physically build, serve, and maintain the country aren't disproportionately funding it.

- **Support small business over corporate welfare.** Corporate subsidies, targeted tax breaks, and preferential treatment for large companies at the expense of small competitors distort markets and concentrate power. We support eliminating industry-specific tax carve-outs and replacing them with broad, neutral incentives available to any business that invests in American workers or infrastructure.

---

### PILLAR THREE: Intellectual and Moral Honesty in Governance

**The Problem:**
American political culture rewards confidence over correctness, soundbites over substance, and tribal loyalty over honest reckoning. Politicians routinely advocate for policies they know to be ineffective because changing position is framed as weakness. The result is a governing class that optimizes for winning arguments rather than solving problems.

**AWFixer Policy Positions:**

- **Evidence-based policy, not ideology-based policy.** Every major policy proposal should include a clear statement of the problem being solved, the mechanism by which the proposed solution addresses it, and measurable outcomes that will be tracked over time. Policies that don't work should be revised or ended, regardless of which party proposed them.

- **Institutional term limits and rotation.** Career politicians develop incentives that diverge from those of their constituents over time. We support reasonable term limits for congressional leadership roles (not necessarily rank-and-file seats) and mandatory rotation off committee assignments to prevent the accumulation of entrenched power.

- **Mandatory conflict-of-interest disclosure.** Any elected official or senior appointee who votes on, regulates, or oversees an industry in which they hold financial interests should be required to divest or publicly disclose those interests before taking any relevant action.

- **Honest language in legislation.** Bills should be named for what they do, not for what sounds good. A bill that loosens environmental oversight should not be called the "Clean Future Act." We support a nonpartisan legislative plain-language review office empowered to flag misleading bill titles and summaries.
