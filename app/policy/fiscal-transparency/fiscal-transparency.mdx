## The Public Ledger — Radical Transparency in Federal Spending

**PILLAR SEVEN: Fiscal Transparency**

### The AWFixer Core Argument

The federal government spent approximately $6.5 trillion in fiscal year 2023. The average American citizen has essentially no meaningful visibility into where that money went, who authorized each expenditure, what it was supposed to accomplish, or whether it did.

This is not acceptable in 2026. The technology to make every dollar of public spending visible, traceable, and permanently recorded exists right now. We use it to track cryptocurrency transactions. There is no technical reason the federal budget cannot operate on the same principle of radical, immutable transparency.

The only reason it doesn't is that opacity is useful to the people who benefit from it.

---

### The Model — Blockchain-Style Transparency

The core innovation is the **distributed, immutable, publicly verifiable record:**

- Every appropriation, expenditure, contract award, grant, transfer, and payment is recorded as a permanent, timestamped entry
- Every entry is linked to the authorizing legislation, the authorizing official, the recipient, and the stated purpose
- The ledger is **public by default** — accessible without FOIA requests, without redaction except narrowly defined national security categories
- The ledger is **immutable** — entries cannot be deleted. Corrections are recorded as new entries
- The ledger is **distributed** — maintained across multiple independent nodes

---

### What Gets Recorded

**Every dollar. No exceptions outside a narrow national security carve-out.**

- Federal budget line items linked to bill text and voting records
- Every contract award above $1,000 — vendor, amount, purpose, contracting officer
- Every grant awarded — applicant pool, selection criteria, reviewing officials
- Congressional spending including earmarks
- Lobbying connections to contract awards

---

### The National Security Carve-Out

- Classified expenditures recorded in aggregate form
- Independent Inspector General with full ledger access reviews classifications
- Automatic declassification after 10 years
- The existence of a classified program cannot itself be classified

---

### Implementation — Federal Public Ledger Authority

AWFixer proposes a **Federal Public Ledger Authority (FPLA)** — an independent agency with:

- Design and maintenance of ledger infrastructure
- Open-source, publicly accessible, searchable interface
- API access for journalists and researchers
- Uniform reporting standards across agencies
- Inspector General with subpoena power
- Real-time anomaly detection

---

### Why This Changes Everything

- **Changes incentive structure** — when every expenditure is visible, officials must ask "can I defend this?"
- **Enables democratic accountability** — 330 million Americans scrutinizing spending in real time
- **Creates feedback loop** — citizens see what their taxes buy
- **Forces honest accounting** — failed programs can no longer be quietly renewed
- **Restores public trust** — the basic condition for fixing governance problems
