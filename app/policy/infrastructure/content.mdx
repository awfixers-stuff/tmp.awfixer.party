## Infrastructure Policy

**Case Study: A Century of Underinvestment**

In 1919, a young Army officer named Dwight Eisenhower joined a military convoy crossing the United States by road. It took 62 days. The roads were so poor that vehicles broke down constantly.

That experience stayed with him. Decades later, as President, he signed the Federal-Aid Highway Act of 1956, creating the Interstate Highway System — one of the most consequential public investments in American history.

Today, the American Society of Civil Engineers gives U.S. infrastructure a C- grade. Bridges, water systems, rail, and roads are aging and underfunded. Meanwhile, countries like China, Japan, South Korea, and much of Western Europe have made sustained infrastructure investments measurable in productivity, quality of life, and economic competitiveness.

America once built the Panama Canal, the Hoover Dam, and the Interstate System. It now struggles to repair the bridges it already has.

**AWFixer Infrastructure Positions:**

- **Reverse the underinvestment.** The postwar consensus that public investment in shared infrastructure was a national good has eroded.

- **Reject the assumption that private capital will fill the gap.** Infrastructure that serves everyone equally is rarely as profitable as infrastructure that serves the wealthy selectively.

- **Sustained investment commitment.** America needs a new generation of infrastructure projects: broadband, rail, water systems, and modernized grid infrastructure.

- **Learn from history.** Public investment in infrastructure is one of the highest-ROI uses of public capital.
